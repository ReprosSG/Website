Switzerland

Regional Hub Western European Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

Switzerland operates under a federal system with 26 cantons, each possessing significant legislative and judicial autonomy. The Swiss legal system blends elements of civil law — rooted in the Swiss Civil Code of 1907 and the Swiss Criminal Code of 1937 — with federal procedural rules harmonised through the Swiss Code of Criminal Procedure of 2011. The Federal Supreme Court (Tribunal fédéral), seated in Lausanne, is the highest court in the country and serves as the ultimate guardian of constitutional rights and the consistency of federal law.

The Swiss criminal justice system is governed by the principle of proportionality and a strong presumption of innocence. Procedural safeguards include the right to a speedy trial, the right to legal representation, and the right to appeal to cantonal and federal levels. The system is accusatorial, with a clear separation between the prosecution — led by public prosecutors — and the judiciary. Switzerland has progressively expanded suspended sentences and community service as alternatives to imprisonment, reflecting a strong rehabilitation ethos.

Switzerland recorded 53 intentional homicides in 2023, corresponding to a rate of 0.60 per 100,000 population — a slight increase from 0.48 in 2022 but consistent with the country's historically low levels of lethal violence (Swiss Federal Statistical Office / UNODC, 2023). The World Bank Rule of Law Index 2024 scores Switzerland at 1.76 out of 2.5, placing it comfortably among the world's most law-abiding societies.

Corruption & Accountability

Switzerland scores 81 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 5th globally (ex-æquo with Norway and Luxembourg). Despite this strong position, the score represents a one-point decline from 2023, which Transparency International attributes to unresolved weaknesses in lobbying regulation, the revolving door between the private sector and public office, and the limited scope of beneficial ownership transparency for Swiss legal entities.

Switzerland's reputation as a global financial centre simultaneously strengthens and challenges its anti-corruption posture. While the country's banking secrecy framework has been substantially reformed since 2018 through the Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI) agreement, concerns remain about its role as a destination for illicit financial flows. The Money Laundering Reporting Office Switzerland (MROS) and the Office of the Attorney General of Switzerland (OAG) are the key national authorities responsible for investigating corruption and financial crime at the federal level.
