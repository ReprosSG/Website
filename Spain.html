Spain

Reino de España Civil Law — Southern Europe

The Judicial Framework

Spain operates a civil law system under the 1978 Constitution, which dismantled the Francoist legal order and established a parliamentary monarchy with a separation of powers model heavily influenced by the German Basic Law. The judicial architecture is tiered across Juzgados de Primera Instancia (first instance courts), Audiencias Provinciales (provincial courts), Tribunales Superiores de Justicia (superior courts of the autonomous communities), the Audiencia Nacional (National High Court with jurisdiction over terrorism, drug trafficking, and large-scale fraud), and at the apex, the Tribunal Supremo — Spain's highest court in all judicial orders except constitutional matters. The Consejo General del Poder Judicial (CGPJ), the judiciary's governing body composed of 20 members appointed by Parliament, is constitutionally mandated to guarantee judicial independence — though its frequent political deadlocks, including a four-year vacancy crisis resolved only in 2021 and further governance disputes in 2024, are widely cited as a structural vulnerability of the Spanish judicial system.

Spain's constitutional judiciary experienced its most polarising episode in decades with the Amnesty Law (Ley Orgánica 1/2024 de amnistía) passed in June 2024 by the Sánchez government as part of his parliamentary pact with Catalan independence parties — granting amnesty for acts related to the 2017 Catalan independence referendum, including to former Catalan president Carles Puigdemont. The Constitutional Court (Tribunal Constitucional) upheld the constitutionality of the general amnesty framework in its landmark ruling STC 137/2025 (published November 2025), while simultaneously annulling specific provisions it deemed unconstitutional. The Constitutional Court of Andalusia challenged these rulings via recurso de inconstitucionalidad 6575-2024, and the BOE published Sentencia 177/2025 in November 2025 confirming the partial validity of the amnesty law. The rulings deepened Spain's territorial and judicial divisions. In September 2024, Isabel Perelló was appointed President of the Tribunal Supremo and CGPJ — the first woman to hold the position in Spain's history.

Spain recorded 331 intentional homicides in 2023 — a rate of 0.69 per 100,000 population, essentially stable from 0.68 in 2022 and representing one of the lowest homicide rates in the EU and globally. This figure has remained consistently low for over a decade, fluctuating between 289 (2018) and 385 (2011), reflecting Spain's highly effective public security apparatus and low levels of organised crime violence by international standards. Of the 331 homicides in 2023, 111 victims (33.5%) were women — reflecting Spain's persistent domestic violence crisis — and 69 occurred within family contexts. Spain has invested heavily in femicide prevention since 2004's landmark Ley Integral contra la Violencia de Género, making it one of the first countries to legally define and specifically prosecute gender-based violence. Eurostat data confirms Spain's 0.69/100k rate is well below the EU average of 0.9/100k recorded in 2023.

Corruption & Accountability

Spain scores 56 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 46th globally — unchanged in score from 2023 (also 56) but falling two places in rank. The 2025 CPI (published February 2026) confirmed a further deterioration: score dropped to 55/100, rank fell to 49th — a loss of three places in one year, placing Spain below Portugal (56) and Slovenia (58) within the EU, as reported by The Diplomat in Spain. Trading Economics confirms Spain's CPI 2025 score at 55/100. The European Commission's 2024 Rule of Law Report for Spain highlights persistent concerns about the length of major corruption trials, limited transparency in political party financing, and the revolving door between regulatory positions and the private sector.

Spain's anti-corruption landscape is shaped by the Oficina Antifraude (in Catalonia), the Agencia Estatal Antifraude, and specialised prosecutors including the Fiscalía Anticorrupción. However, the country remains haunted by systemic corruption scandals: the PP (Partido Popular) Gürtel case — which resulted in Spain's first-ever conviction of a governing party for institutional corruption in 2018 — set a historic precedent, but subsequent cases involving the PP, PSOE, and regional parties in Valencia, Murcia, and Andalusia have continued to erode public trust. The Caso Koldo (2024), involving alleged commissions paid for medical supplies during the COVID-19 pandemic to a network linked to Socialist politicians, was the defining corruption affair of the Sánchez legislative term. Spain ranks 17th out of 27 EU member states in the CPI 2025 — a position that Transparency International Spain describes as "far from the top tier of EU performers."

Institutional Landmark
Tribunal Supremo de España — Madrid

Le Tribunal Supremo d'Espagne, inauguré en 1882 dans l'ancien couvent des Agustinos Recoletos du quartier de Las Salesas Reales à Madrid, est la juridiction suprême de l'ordre judiciaire espagnol en matière civile, pénale, contentieux-administrative, sociale et militaire. Sa façade néoclassique ornée d'un groupe sculpté couronné du drapeau espagnol — et son fronton monumental aux colonnes de granit — symbolise la permanence de l'État de droit dans une démocratie constitutionnelle confrontée à ses tensions territoriales et institutionnelles les plus profondes depuis 1978.
