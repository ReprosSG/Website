Sweden

The Judicial Framework

Sweden's legal system is rooted in civil law tradition, with a strong emphasis on transparency, openness, and individual rights. The Freedom of the Press Act — first enacted in 1766 and one of the oldest in the world — remains a cornerstone of Swedish constitutional law. The judicial system is structured in three tiers: district courts (tingsrätter), courts of appeal (hovrätter), and the Supreme Court (Högsta domstolen), seated in Stockholm's Gamla Stan. According to the World Justice Project Rule of Law Index 2024, Sweden ranks 4th out of 142 countries globally, with particularly strong scores in civil justice, absence of corruption, and open government.

The Swedish criminal justice system balances deterrence with a pronounced rehabilitation philosophy. Correctional services are managed by the Swedish Prison and Probation Service (Kriminalvården), which operates under a mandate to reduce reoffending through education, vocational training, and structured reintegration programmes. Sweden has also invested significantly in preventive justice, with diversion programmes and restorative justice measures used widely for first-time and juvenile offenders.

Sweden recorded an intentional homicide rate of 1.08 per 100,000 population in 2023 (UNODC), a figure that, while still low by global standards, reflects a notable increase compared to earlier years — partly attributed to gang-related shootings in urban areas. The Swedish government has responded with targeted policing reforms, expanded use of surveillance, and tougher sentencing for gang crimes, while maintaining the broader rehabilitation framework of the correctional system.

Corruption & Accountability

Sweden scores 80 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 6th globally with no change from 2023. The country's anti-corruption framework is reinforced by a powerful combination of judicial independence, a free and investigative media, and a tradition of open government records. The Swedish Anti-Corruption Institute and the Parliamentary Ombudsman (JO) provide robust oversight of public administration and the conduct of public officials.

The EU Rule of Law Report 2024 commends Sweden for its high level of judicial independence, noting that public trust in courts remains strong and that the process for appointing judges is transparent and merit-based. However, the report flags concerns around whistleblower protection mechanisms and calls for more systematic anti-corruption training across public institutions — areas where Sweden's framework, while solid, has room for improvement.

Institutional Landmark
Stockholm Court House — Stockholm, Sweden

The Stockholm Court House, inaugurated in 1915, is one of Sweden's most distinguished judicial landmarks. Its distinctive green copper dome, red-tiled roof, and arched windows embody the Swedish tradition of transparent and accessible justice.
