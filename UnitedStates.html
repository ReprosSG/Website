The Judicial Framework

The United States operates one of the world's most complex legal systems: a federal common law structure in which judicial authority is shared between the federal government and fifty state governments, each maintaining its own court hierarchy, procedural rules, and substantive law. At the federal level, the judicial branch comprises 94 District Courts as courts of first instance, thirteen Courts of Appeals organised by regional circuit, and the Supreme Court of the United States — the apex of both the federal judiciary and the ultimate constitutional arbiter for all American law. The Supreme Court consists of nine Justices — the Chief Justice and eight Associate Justices — appointed for life by the President with Senate confirmation, making its composition a defining battleground of American political life.

The Supreme Court's 2024 term (October 2024–October 2025) was among the most consequential in recent decades. Key rulings included decisions on birthright citizenship limits sought by the Trump administration (Trump v. CASA, Inc.), transgender healthcare policy (United States v. Skrmetti), and multiple cases touching executive power and administrative law. Chief Justice John G. Roberts Jr., appointed in 2005, presides over a court whose 6-3 conservative majority — cemented by the three Trump-era appointments of Gorsuch (2017), Kavanaugh (2018), and Barrett (2020) — has reshaped American constitutional law across abortion, gun rights, administrative deference, and executive immunity over the past four years.

The United States recorded 20,162 homicide deaths in 2024 — a rate of 5.9 per 100,000 population according to CDC National Vital Statistics data, the lowest in nine years but still dramatically higher than all other G7 nations. Of those, 15,364 (76%) were firearm homicides, a rate of 4.5 per 100,000 — reflecting the unique severity of America's gun violence epidemic. Encouragingly, FBI data shows intentional homicides fell by 14.9% in 2024 vs. 2023, the largest single-year decline recorded since modern tracking began, continuing a sharp reversal of the pandemic-era surge that peaked in 2020 at 7.8 per 100,000.

Corruption & Accountability

The United States scores 64 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 28th globally — a one-point decline from 2023 and a sustained erosion from its historical peak of 74 in 2016. This places the US below all other G7 nations and below countries including Uruguay (74), Estonia (76), and Chile (67). Transparency International USA attributes the decline to deepening concerns about dark money in political financing, weakened federal ethics enforcement, persistent influence of corporate lobbying on legislative and regulatory processes, and growing international attention to the role of US real estate and financial services in facilitating foreign corruption and money laundering.

The United States' anti-corruption enforcement architecture is among the most powerful globally in scope: the Department of Justice (DOJ), the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC), the FBI, and the Treasury Department's Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) collectively conduct the world's most significant foreign bribery enforcement through the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA), which has generated over $30 billion in penalties since 2010. However, domestic political corruption remains structurally difficult to prosecute, as the Supreme Court has progressively narrowed federal corruption statutes — notably in McDonnell v. United States (2016) and Snyder v. United States (2024), which significantly limited the reach of honest services fraud and gratuities law respectively. The intersection of money in politics, revolving-door lobbying, and weakened ethics oversight constitutes what TI USA describes as the "structural corruption" challenge of American democracy.

Institutional Landmark
Supreme Court of the United States — Washington, D.C.

The Supreme Court Building on Capitol Hill, completed in 1935 and designed by Cass Gilbert in the Corinthian neoclassical style, bears the inscription "Equal Justice Under Law" above its iconic marble colonnade. It serves as the final arbiter of the United States Constitution and federal law, whose nine life-tenured Justices have shaped American society on every fundamental question from civil rights to executive power.
