The Judicial Framework

India operates a common law system inherited from British colonial rule, governed by the Constitution of India (1950) — the longest written constitution of any sovereign nation, at over 145,000 words. The judicial hierarchy runs from District Courts and Sessions Courts through 25 High Courts (one for each state or union territory grouping) to the apex Supreme Court of India on Tilak Marg in New Delhi — a body of 34 judges, including the Chief Justice of India, exercising original, appellate, and advisory jurisdiction under Articles 131–147. India's Supreme Court is one of the most powerful in the world: its Basic Structure Doctrine (established in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala, 1973) holds that no parliament can amend the constitution in ways that destroy its fundamental character — a principle that has survived over five decades of political challenge and remains the cornerstone of Indian constitutional law. Appointment of Supreme Court and High Court judges occurs through the Collegium system — a five-judge Supreme Court committee that recommends appointments — a process widely criticised for its opacity.

In a landmark judgment delivered on 20 May 2025 — exactly one year before this report — the Supreme Court of India introduced comprehensive reforms to the Indian Judicial Services, restructuring qualification, promotion, and selection processes for lower judiciary officers across all states. The judgment restored the 25% LDCE promotion quota for the Higher Judicial Service, reduced minimum qualifying service for accelerated promotion from five to three years, and mandated a suitability test for merit-cum-seniority promotions. In 2025, the Court's engagement with constitutional governance was exceptional in scope: it ruled on the powers of state governors to withhold assent to bills (State of Tamil Nadu v Governor), affirmed that anti-defection Speakers have no immunity from judicial review (State of Telangana), and invalidated executive practices that undermined tribunal independence (Union of India v Madras Bar Association). The International Commission of Jurists (ICJ) published a February 2025 report warning that judicial independence in India faces structural threats from executive influence over appointments, selective transfers of inconvenient judges, and a pattern of contempt proceedings against critics.

India registered 27,049 murders in 2024 — a 2.4% decline from 27,721 in 2023 — according to the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) Crime in India 2024 report published in May 2026. The murder rate stands at approximately 1.9 per 100,000 population — low by global standards given India's 1.44 billion population, though the absolute number of homicides is the fourth highest in the world. The NCRB identifies "disputes" as the leading motive (32.4%), followed by personal vendetta and economic gain. India's overall crime rate declined 6% in 2024, from 448.3 to 418.9 incidents per lakh population — a broadly positive trend. However, crimes against women declined only 1.5%, and offences against the state rose 6.6%, reflecting the political repression concerns raised by civil liberties organisations.

Corruption & Accountability

India scores 38 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 96th globally — a decline of one point from 39 in 2023 (rank 93rd) and 40 in 2022 (rank 85th), representing a three-year consecutive deterioration. This downward trajectory places India below all its BRICS peers except Russia and below regional neighbours such as Sri Lanka (121st) — though above China (76th), Pakistan (135th), and Bangladesh (149th). India's CPI score reflects perceived corruption in the public sector, with particular concerns around electoral financing opacity, land acquisition corruption, and politicised use of enforcement agencies (CBI, ED, IT Department) against opposition politicians — a pattern documented by Transparency International India and the ICJ's 2025 judicial independence report.

India's anti-corruption framework centres on the Central Vigilance Commission (CVC), the Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG), the Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI), and the Enforcement Directorate (ED) — all of which operate under executive supervision and have been criticised for selective prosecution. The Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act 2013 established an independent anti-corruption ombudsman, but the Lokpal's institutional effectiveness has been limited by under-resourcing and a narrow investigative mandate. India's largest recent corruption case — the ₹20,000 crore (approx. USD 2.4 billion) Electoral Bonds scheme, struck down as unconstitutional by the Supreme Court in February 2024 — exposed systemic links between corporate donations and government contracts, forcing the State Bank of India to disclose all bond purchasers for the first time. The judgment was widely hailed as the most consequential anti-corruption ruling in India in a generation.

Institutional Landmark
Supreme Court of India — Tilak Marg, New Delhi

La Cour suprême de l'Inde, inaugurée en 1958 sur Tilak Marg à New Delhi, est le plus haut tribunal du pays et le gardien de la Constitution de 1950 — la plus longue constitution écrite au monde. Son architecture néo-classique mêlant dôme central blanc et grès rouge du Rajasthan (le même matériau que les grands édifices coloniaux de New Delhi conçus par Edwin Lutyens) abrite 34 juges dont le Chief Justice of India. La Cour a posé en 1973 la doctrine de la "Basic Structure" — parmi les doctrines constitutionnelles les plus influentes d'Asie — protégeant l'essence de la Constitution contre toute révision parlementaire.
