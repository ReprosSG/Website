Seychelles

Republic of Seychelles African Island Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

Seychelles operates a mixed legal system drawing from both English common law and French civil law traditions, a dual inheritance reflecting the archipelago's successive colonial histories under France (1742–1810) and Britain (1810–1976). The 1993 Constitution of the Republic of Seychelles guarantees judicial independence and enshrines the separation of powers as a foundational principle. The judicial hierarchy comprises the Magistrates' Courts at the base, the Supreme Court presided over by the Chief Justice, and the Court of Appeal as the apex court — all seated in Victoria, Mahé, the world's smallest capital city. Seychelles is a member of the Eastern Africa Court of Justice and participates in the Southern and Eastern African Chief Justices' Leadership Forum, which met in Victoria in April 2025.

Seychelles has pursued active legislative modernisation in recent years. In September 2024, the National Assembly passed the Penal Code (Amendment) Act 2024, introducing hate crime legislation for the first time in the country's history. The Act — notably the first in the world to adopt a "hybrid model" combining aggravated offences with hate speech provisions — demonstrates Seychelles' commitment to aligning its criminal justice framework with international human rights standards. The Seychelles Legal Information Institute (SeyLII) provides free public access to all court judgments, reinforcing transparency and the rule of law through open justice principles.

Seychelles presents a paradox in crime data: despite its exceptional governance scores, the archipelago records a relatively high homicide rate of approximately 12.7 per 100,000 population (UNODC, 2016, latest available), a figure that places it above many peer nations and reflects the complex socio-economic dynamics of small island states, including drug trafficking through Indian Ocean routes and concentrated urban poverty. The Royal Seychelles Police Force has cited significant ongoing efforts in anti-narcotics operations and community policing to address these structural challenges.

Corruption & Accountability

Seychelles scores 72 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 18th globally and achieving the highest score ever recorded by any Sub-Saharan African country. This result also represents the largest CPI improvement globally over the past decade, with Seychelles gaining 20 points since 2012 — a testament to a sustained, whole-of-government anti-corruption reform programme spanning multiple administrations. The result places Seychelles comfortably ahead of the next best African performers: Cape Verde (62), Rwanda (57), and Botswana (57).

Seychelles' anti-corruption architecture is anchored by the Anti-Corruption Commission of Seychelles (ACCS), established under the Anti-Corruption Act of 2016, which has pursued high-profile prosecutions including the landmark Valabhji case — a major money laundering and asset recovery proceeding active in 2024. President Wavel Ramkalawan, elected in 2020, has made institutional reform and transparency a central pillar of his administration, championing initiatives such as the Beneficial Ownership Register and mandatory declarations of assets for public officials. The Government of Seychelles officially welcomed the 2024 CPI result as a "milestone that belongs to every Seychellois."

Institutional Landmark
Magistrates Court — Victoria, Mahé, Seychelles

The Magistrates Court of Seychelles, inaugurated in Victoria, reflects the country's investment in modern judicial infrastructure. With its imposing white columns and contemporary glass entrance, the building signals a deliberate shift toward accessible, transparent justice — a physical embodiment of Seychelles' decade-long institutional reform drive that propelled the nation to 18th place globally in the 2024 Corruption Perceptions Index.
