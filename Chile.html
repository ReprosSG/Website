Chile

República de Chile Civil Law — Latin America

The Judicial Framework

Chile operates a civil law system rooted in the Spanish colonial tradition and the Napoleonic codification wave that swept Latin America in the 19th century. The Código Civil, drafted by jurist Andrés Bello and promulgated in 1855, remains in force today and has been enormously influential across the region, serving as the direct model for the civil codes of Ecuador, Colombia, El Salvador, Honduras, and Nicaragua. The judicial hierarchy runs from Juzgados (lower courts) through 17 Cortes de Apelaciones (Courts of Appeal) to the Corte Suprema (Supreme Court) in Santiago — a 21-member body whose justices serve until the mandatory retirement age of 75. Uniquely in the region, the Chilean Supreme Court lacks constitutional review powers and cannot invalidate legislation — that function is reserved for the Tribunal Constitucional (Constitutional Tribunal), a separate body created in 1970 and reinstated under the 1980 Constitution, which performs a priori review of legislation before promulgation.

Chile's Supreme Court made international headlines in January 2024 with a landmark pro-arbitration ruling, granting exequatur to a foreign arbitration award and reaffirming the country's commitment to the New York Convention and international commercial arbitration. This ruling reinforced Chile's reputation as the most arbitration-friendly jurisdiction in Latin America and a regional hub for commercial dispute resolution. Chile's current judicial reform agenda is dominated by the ongoing constitutional process: after the rejection of the 2022 draft constitution (which would have fundamentally restructured the judiciary) and the subsequent 2023 draft also failing at referendum, the country remains under the 1980 constitution with incremental reforms, and the Boric government continues to pursue targeted judicial modernisation measures — including digital court proceedings and a reform of the Tribunal Constitucional's composition and jurisdiction.

Chile recorded 1,248 intentional homicides in 2023 — a rate of 6.35 per 100,000 population, down from the record 6.80 per 100,000 in 2022 but representing a 144% increase from 2010's rate of approximately 2.32 per 100,000. The Fiscalía de Chile's 2023 homicide report identifies organised crime as the primary driver: 37% of all homicides are linked to criminal organisations or occurred in the context of other offences, with firearms used in 52% of cases. Metropolitan Santiago concentrates 18% of victims, with particularly affected communes including Santiago Centro, Puente Alto, and San Bernardo. The sharp rise in violence since 2016 is largely attributed to the arrival of transnational criminal networks — particularly Venezuelan and Colombian organisations involved in drug trafficking — exploiting migration flows and Chile's historically under-resourced border control infrastructure.

Corruption & Accountability

Chile scores 63 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 31st globally — unchanged in score from 2023 but dropping three positions in rank, reflecting improvements by peer nations. Steptoe analysis notes Chile remains the least corrupt country in Latin America, ranking above Uruguay (76 points) — though Uruguay consistently outperforms in raw score — and far ahead of regional peers Brazil (34), Mexico (26), and Colombia (40). Chile's strong institutional framework, independent judiciary, and functioning audit institutions underpin its regional leadership, though the country faces growing pressure from high-profile corruption scandals in the private sector and municipal government.

Chile's primary anti-corruption bodies include the Contraloría General de la República, an independent constitutional body that audits government spending and legality of executive action, and the Fiscalía de Chile's dedicated economic crime units. The most significant recent corruption case is the ongoing Caso Convenios investigation, which emerged in 2023 and revealed systemic misuse of state social programme funds channelled through NGOs to allied political organisations — implicating officials across multiple municipalities and the government of President Boric, who dismissed several ministers in response. The scandal, involving hundreds of millions of pesos in diverted funds, has significantly damaged public trust in the executive and prompted emergency legislative reforms to NGO funding and transparency requirements, passed in late 2024.

Institutional Landmark
Palacio de los Tribunales de Justicia — Santiago

Le Palacio de los Tribunales de Justicia, inauguré en 1930 sur la Plaza Montt-Varas au cœur de Santiago, abrite la Corte Suprema de Chile et la Corte de Apelaciones de Santiago. Son architecture néoclassique — colonnes corinthiennes, fronton triangulaire et drapeau chilien — en fait l'un des édifices institutionnels les plus emblématiques du continent sud-américain. Ses 21 ministres y rendent des arrêts définitifs en matière civile et pénale, sans pouvoir d'annulation des lois, une originalité constitutionnelle unique en Amérique latine.
