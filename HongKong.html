Hong Kong

Special Administrative Region People's Republic of China

The Judicial Framework

Hong Kong operates under a common law system rooted in English legal tradition, preserved under the constitutional principle of "one country, two systems" as enshrined in the Basic Law enacted by the National People's Congress of China. The judicial hierarchy consists of the Magistrates' Courts, District Court, High Court (comprising the Court of First Instance and the Court of Appeal), and the Court of Final Appeal (CFA) — the apex court, which retains the unique authority to invite judges from common law jurisdictions overseas, including the United Kingdom, Australia, and Canada, to sit on its bench. This practice, designed to reinforce judicial independence and international legal credibility, is unparalleled among Chinese jurisdictions.

Hong Kong's legal system is administered entirely independently of the executive and legislative authorities, as guaranteed by Article 85 of the Basic Law. The Department of Justice oversees public prosecutions, while the Judiciary is entirely self-governing under the Chief Justice of the Court of Final Appeal. The 2024 Judiciary Annual Report notes steady progress in reducing case backlogs following the disruptions of 2019–2022, with court efficiency and digitalisation identified as key priorities for the coming years.

Hong Kong records one of the world's lowest homicide rates. Official police statistics recorded just 28 homicide cases in 2023, representing a rate of approximately 0.37 per 100,000 population — among the lowest globally for any major metropolitan jurisdiction. The 2024 crime figures show a further decline to 5 homicide cases in the first reporting period, reflecting Hong Kong's enduring status as one of the world's safest cities despite the social tensions of recent years.

Corruption & Accountability

Hong Kong scores 74 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 14th globally — a two-point increase from 2023 and one of the strongest performances in the Asia-Pacific region. This result reflects the continued effectiveness of the Independent Commission Against Corruption (ICAC), established in 1974, which transformed Hong Kong from a society rife with systemic police and government corruption into one of the most corruption-free jurisdictions in the world. The ICAC combines law enforcement, prevention, and community education functions in a uniquely integrated anti-corruption model widely studied and replicated internationally.

The World Justice Project Rule of Law Index 2024 ranks Hong Kong 23rd globally out of 142 jurisdictions, 6th in East Asia and the Pacific, with particularly strong scores in the absence of corruption (10th globally) and regulatory enforcement. The HKSAR Government notes that Hong Kong's scores in constraints on government powers and fundamental rights show slight declines, attributing these to the implementation of the National Security Law (2020) and the Safeguarding National Security Ordinance (2024), which have attracted international scrutiny regarding civil liberties and judicial independence in sensitive cases.

Institutional Landmark
Court of Final Appeal — Hong Kong, Central

The Court of Final Appeal Building, formerly the Old Supreme Court Building (1912), stands at the heart of Central district surrounded by modern skyscrapers. Its neoclassical colonnade and dome embody Hong Kong's enduring common law heritage and judicial independence under "one country, two systems".
