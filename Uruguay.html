Uruguay

Regional Leader South American Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

Uruguay operates under a civil law system of Napoleonic tradition, anchored by the Constitution of 1967 (with amendments) and a codified legal framework covering civil, criminal, and administrative law. The judiciary is entirely independent from the executive and legislative branches under Article 233 of the Constitution, with the Supreme Court of Justice (Suprema Corte de Justicia) serving as the highest judicial authority. The Court has five members elected by a two-thirds majority of the General Assembly for 10-year non-renewable terms, a mechanism specifically designed to insulate the judiciary from political cycles.

Uruguay's criminal justice system underwent a fundamental reform with the introduction of the Accusatory Criminal Process Code (Código del Proceso Penal) in 2017, replacing the old inquisitorial system with a modern adversarial model. The reform introduced oral public trials, a clear separation between investigative and judicial functions, and stronger guarantees for defendants' rights. Uruguay was also the first country in Latin America to fully ratify and implement the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court, reflecting its deep commitment to international human rights law.

Uruguay recorded a homicide rate of approximately 11.8 per 100,000 population in 2023 (UNODC), driven largely by organised crime and drug trafficking activity in Montevideo and border regions with Brazil. While high by European standards, this rate represents a significant regional outlier compared to neighbouring countries, and the government has deployed targeted security reinforcements and social prevention programmes to address the issue without compromising civil liberties.

Corruption & Accountability

Uruguay scores 76 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 13th globally — a three-point increase from 2023 and the highest score ever recorded by a Latin American country. This result places Uruguay ahead of Canada (15th), the United States (28th), and all other countries in the Western Hemisphere, making it the undisputed transparency leader of the Americas and a benchmark for institutional integrity in the region.

Uruguay's clean governance reputation is built on a foundation of strong state institutions, robust civil society oversight, a free press, and a long tradition of meritocratic public administration. The country has maintained a consistent anti-corruption culture across political cycles, reinforced by asset declaration laws for public officials, an independent judiciary, and a dedicated anti-corruption unit within the Attorney General's Office (Fiscalía General de la Nación). Uruguay's progressive social policies — including legalisation of cannabis, same-sex marriage, and universal healthcare — reflect a broader commitment to transparent, rights-based governance.
