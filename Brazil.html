Brazil

República Federativa do Brasil Civil Law — Latin America

The Judicial Framework

Brazil operates a civil law system of the Romano-Germanic tradition, codified through the Código Civil (2002) and Código de Processo Penal (1941, extensively amended), governed by the Constitution of 1988 — the so-called "Cidadã" (Citizen's) Constitution — which re-established democracy after 21 years of military dictatorship and created one of the most expansive bills of rights in the world. Brazil's judicial architecture is uniquely complex: the federal judiciary encompasses the Supreme Federal Court (STF), the Superior Court of Justice (STJ), specialised Superior Courts (TST for labour, TSE for electoral, STM for military), federal courts of appeal, and first-instance federal judges — while parallel state court systems handle the vast majority of ordinary civil and criminal matters. The STF — housed in the iconic Oscar Niemeyer modernist palace on Praça dos Três Poderes in Brasília — consists of 11 justices appointed by the President with Senate confirmation, serving until mandatory retirement at age 75.

Brazil's STF was at the epicentre of the country's democratic crisis in 2024–2025. On 18 December 2024, the court convicted former President Jair Bolsonaro — who had attempted to retain power following his October 2022 electoral defeat — to 27 years and three months in prison for leading a coup plot that included the January 8, 2023 attacks on the Presidential Palace, Congress, and the STF itself. The conviction, handed down by eight votes to three, marked the first time in Brazilian history that a former head of state was criminally convicted, and triggered massive protests from Bolsonaro's base. Chief Justice Luís Roberto Barroso, who presided over the proceeding, was replaced as Chief Justice by Edson Fachin in September 2025 — with Alexandre de Moraes serving as vice-president of the court. The STF ended 2024 with its lowest case backlog in 30 years, having issued over 114,000 decisions during the year, according to Mattos Filho.

Brazil recorded 44,127 homicides in 2024 — the lowest total since 2012 — representing a 5.4% decrease compared to 2023 (46,645), according to the Brazilian Forum on Public Security (FBSP) report published July 2025. The homicide rate fell from 19.26 to 18.21 per 100,000 population — but Brazil remains the country with the highest absolute number of homicides in the world, accounting for approximately 12% of all global murders. Of the 44,127 total violent deaths, Sinesp data identifies 35,642 as intentional homicides, 1,438 as femicides, and 924 as robberies followed by death. The states of Bahia (4,480), Rio de Janeiro (3,504), Pernambuco (3,381), Ceará (3,272), and Minas Gerais (3,042) concentrate the greatest absolute numbers. Police killings remain alarming: 6,121 people were killed by police in 2024 — 17 per day — a 4.2% reduction from 2023 but a figure that dwarfs police lethality in any democratic country globally.

Corruption & Accountability

Brazil scores 34 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — its worst score since TI first assessed Brazil in 2012 (when it scored 43), ranking 107th out of 180 countries. This represents a two-point, three-position decline from 2023 (36/100, rank 104), placing Brazil below the global average (43) and the Americas regional average (42), and at the same level as Algeria, Malawi, Nepal, Niger, Thailand, and Turkey. The CPI 2025 (published February 2026) showed a marginal statistical recovery to 35/100 — but rank remained at 107th, and Transparency International Brazil describes the improvement as "statistically insignificant." Brazil's trajectory since 2017 — when it scored 37 in the wake of the Lava Jato anti-corruption operation — has been one of uninterrupted regression, coinciding with the institutionalisation of political resistance to anti-corruption enforcement.

Brazil's Lava Jato (Car Wash) operation (2014–2021) was the largest anti-corruption investigation in Latin American history, recovering over R$4 billion and convicting 174 individuals including former President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva — whose convictions were subsequently annulled by the STF on procedural grounds in 2021, allowing his return to the presidency. The investigation's dismantling, combined with the Bolsonaro government's weakening of federal oversight bodies and the STF's Fachin ruling on venue jurisdiction, effectively ended systematic anti-corruption prosecutorial action at the federal level. Under President Lula's third term (2023–), the Controladoria-Geral da União (CGU) and the Advocacia-Geral da União have resumed asset recovery actions, but major new corruption investigations have been replaced by the STF's democracy-focused docket and Bolsonaro-era coup accountability proceedings.

Institutional Landmark
Supremo Tribunal Federal — Praça dos Três Poderes, Brasília

Le Supremo Tribunal Federal, inauguré en 1960 sur la Praça dos Três Poderes à Brasília, est l'œuvre d'Oscar Niemeyer — l'un des chefs-d'œuvre de l'architecture moderniste mondiale. Ses colonnes incurvées en béton blanc soutenant un toit plat horizontal et son immense façade vitrée incarnent la modernité démocratique du Brésil. En décembre 2024, c'est dans cette enceinte que l'ancien président Jair Bolsonaro a été condamné à 27 ans de prison pour tentative de coup d'État — le premier ex-chef d'État brésilien à être convaincu de crime.
