Taiwan

Republic of China (ROC) Civil Law — East Asia Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

Taiwan's legal system is rooted in the German-influenced civil law tradition, inherited through the Republic of China's legal reforms of the 1920s–1930s. The judiciary is organised under the Judicial Yuan (司法院), one of the five constitutional branches of government — a unique structure deriving from Sun Yat-sen's Five Power Constitution. Under the Judicial Yuan sits a three-tier court system for civil and criminal matters: District Courts (地方法院), High Courts (高等法院), and the Supreme Court (最高法院, Zuìgāo Fǎyuàn) which functions exclusively as a court of cassation, reviewing questions of law rather than fact. Separate from this hierarchy, the Constitutional Court (憲法法庭) — composed of fifteen Grand Justices appointed by the President with Legislative Yuan confirmation for eight-year non-renewable terms — serves as Taiwan's apex constitutional arbiter, with jurisdiction over constitutional interpretation, fundamental rights protection, and review of unconstitutional statutes.

Taiwan's Constitutional Court became the centre of a profound institutional crisis in 2024–2025. The opposition-controlled Legislative Yuan (dominated by the Kuomintang and Taiwan People's Party) passed amendments to the Constitutional Court Procedure Act in late 2024, requiring a quorum of 10 and a supermajority of 9 justices for constitutional rulings — provisions deliberately designed to paralyse the Court, which had only 8 sitting justices after 7 retired simultaneously in October 2024 and two subsequent nomination rounds were blocked by the legislature. On December 19, 2025, the Constitutional Court — convening with just 5 of its 8 remaining justices — struck down these amendments by a 5-3 ruling, holding that the legislature had violated the separation of powers principle and had "obviously and significantly" departed from constitutional requirements in the law's passage. The ruling was immediately contested by the KMT as illegitimate, deepening Taiwan's constitutional stand-off heading into 2026.

Taiwan records one of the lowest homicide rates among all East Asian jurisdictions. In 2023, 291 homicide cases were registered nationally — a rate of approximately 1.22 per 100,000 population, comparable to Japan and significantly lower than South Korea. Taiwan's overall crime rate stood at 1,179 per 100,000 in 2023, with a crime-clearing rate of 97.1% — among the highest in the world — reflecting an efficient and well-resourced national police force. Organised crime (historically tied to the Bamboo Union and Four Seas gangs) has been substantially reduced through sustained law enforcement operations since the 2000s, though illicit narcotics trafficking from Southeast Asia and China remains a persistent enforcement challenge.

Corruption & Accountability

Taiwan scores 68 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2025 (published February 2026), ranking 24th globally — its highest rank ever recorded, up one place from 25th in 2024, and surpassing 86% of all assessed countries. Taiwan's Agency Against Corruption (廉政署) attributes the improvement to sustained institutional reforms including mandatory asset disclosure for officials, strengthened whistleblower protections, and consistent prosecution of public corruption cases under President Lai Ching-te's administration. The score of 68 — up one point from 67 in 2023 and 2024 — places Taiwan squarely in the company of Western European democracies and ahead of all other East Asian jurisdictions except Singapore (84) and Hong Kong (74).

Taiwan's anti-corruption architecture is anchored by the Agency Against Corruption under the Ministry of Justice, which operates a dedicated integrity hotline, conducts proactive integrity testing of civil servants, and manages a comprehensive asset declaration system covering approximately 140,000 public officials. High-profile prosecutions have included former President Chen Shui-bian (convicted of corruption in 2009) and multiple local government officials. The ongoing constitutional crisis — in which the legislature's blocking of judicial appointments is widely perceived as a politically motivated attempt to fill the Court with opposition-aligned justices — represents the most significant structural rule-of-law challenge Taiwan has faced since democratisation, with implications for Taiwan's international standing as a democratic model in Asia.

Institutional Landmark
司法大廈 Hall of Justice — Zhongzheng District, Taipei

The Hall of Justice (司法大廈) in Taipei's Zhongzheng District houses the Judicial Yuan, one of the five constitutional branches of the Republic of China government. Built in the colonial Japanese Baroque style and completed in 1934, it hosts the Supreme Court and the Constitutional Court — the fifteen Grand Justices whose dramatic 2025 self-assertion against legislative interference made global headlines and reaffirmed the independence of constitutional review in Taiwan's maturing democracy.
