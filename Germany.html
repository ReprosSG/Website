The Judicial Framework

Germany operates under a civil law system rooted in the Roman law tradition, codified through the German Civil Code (Bürgerliches Gesetzbuch, BGB) of 1900 and the German Criminal Code (Strafgesetzbuch, StGB). The judicial structure is federal, with each of the 16 Länder maintaining their own courts, capped by five supreme federal courts: the Federal Court of Justice (Bundesgerichtshof, BGH) for civil and criminal matters, the Federal Administrative Court, the Federal Finance Court, the Federal Labour Court, and the Federal Social Court. Above all sits the Federal Constitutional Court (Bundesverfassungsgericht) in Karlsruhe, which serves as the guardian of the Basic Law (Grundgesetz) and holds the power to invalidate legislation incompatible with constitutional rights.

Germany's criminal justice system operates on the principle of legality (Legalitätsprinzip), requiring prosecutors to pursue all criminal cases where sufficient evidence exists — unlike discretionary systems. The system is broadly inquisitorial, with professional judges playing an active role in directing trials and evaluating evidence. A landmark 2024 reform strengthened Germany's framework for universal jurisdiction through the Law for the Development of International Criminal Law, expanding the prosecution of war crimes, crimes against humanity, and genocide regardless of where they were committed — cementing Germany's role as a global leader in international criminal justice.

Germany recorded 661 intentional homicides in 2023, a homicide rate of 0.79 per 100,000 population (Eurostat, 2025) — an increase of 47 cases from 2022, though still among the lowest for a major economy of comparable size. The Federal Criminal Police Office (Bundeskriminalamt, BKA) attributes the slight increase primarily to domestic violence and urban knife crime, while the overall trend of low lethal violence remains firmly established by international standards.

Corruption & Accountability

Germany scores 75 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 14th globally — a two-point decline from 2023 and its lowest score since 2014. Transparency International attributes this drop to unresolved weaknesses in political party financing transparency, slow implementation of anti-corruption recommendations from GRECO, and high-profile lobbying scandals in the Bundestag. The EU Rule of Law Report 2024 echoes these concerns, calling for more stringent rules on the revolving door between politics and the private sector.

Despite these concerns, Germany maintains strong anti-corruption institutions: the Federal Audit Office (Bundesrechnungshof), an independent judiciary, a vigorous free press, and a robust civil society. Germany is also a co-founder of the International Anti-Corruption Academy (IACA) and plays a leading role in EU and OECD anti-corruption initiatives. The 2024 reform of international criminal law further underscores Germany's broader commitment to accountability — applying universal jurisdiction to prosecute war crimes and crimes against humanity committed abroad, regardless of the perpetrator's nationality.

Institutional Landmark
Bundesverfassungsgericht — Federal Constitutional Court, Karlsruhe

Established in 1951 and seated in Karlsruhe, the Federal Constitutional Court is the guardian of Germany's Basic Law (Grundgesetz). Its ornate building with green copper dome has become a symbol of Germany's post-war commitment to constitutional democracy and the rule of law.
