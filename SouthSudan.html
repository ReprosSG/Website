South Sudan

Republic of South Sudan Common Law — East Africa

The Judicial Framework

South Sudan — the world's youngest nation, having declared independence from Sudan on 9 July 2011 — operates a common law system inherited from British colonial administration via Sudan, governed by the Transitional Constitution of the Republic of South Sudan (2011, as amended). The judicial hierarchy runs from Payam Courts and County Courts at the lowest level, through High Courts (one per state), Courts of Appeal, to the Supreme Court of South Sudan in Juba as the apex institution. The Supreme Court exercises original jurisdiction in constitutional matters and final appellate jurisdiction in all other cases. Judges of the Supreme Court and Courts of Appeal are appointed by the Judicial Service Commission on the recommendation of the President — a nomination process that has been chronically politicised, with mass promotions of judges criticised for bypassing merit criteria. Customary courts operating under traditional law remain the primary justice mechanism for the vast majority of the rural population, adjudicating an estimated 80% of all disputes.

South Sudan's judiciary has been operating under a state of near-institutional collapse since the civil war of 2013–2018 and its aftermath. The EU-supported Judicial Reform Committee (JRC), whose recommendations were validated in October 2024, identified 15 critical structural deficiencies: executive interference with judicial appointments and decisions, inadequate infrastructure (many courts operate without electricity or running water), absence of legal aid for defendants, chronic underfunding (the judiciary receives less than 1% of the national budget), and the near-total breakdown of formal justice delivery in conflict-affected states. Responding to this, the Judiciary of South Sudan launched its Strategic Plan 2025–2029 in June 2025 — supported by the EU, the Max Planck Foundation, and UNDP — setting targets for judicial independence, training, and infrastructure. A landmark milestone in 2024 was the deployment of the first mobile court to Leer (Unity State) in over a decade, which resulted in 257 cases being decided — the first formal justice delivered in Unity State in over a year.

Violence against civilians in South Sudan remains at catastrophic levels, despite the 2018 Revitalized Agreement on the Resolution of Conflict (R-ARCSS). UNMISS documented 1,607 civilian victims in Q1 2025 (January–March) — an 86% increase from Q4 2024 (866 victims) and a 76% increase from Q1 2024 (913 victims) — including 739 killed, 679 injured, 149 abducted, and 40 cases of conflict-related sexual violence. Warrap State recorded the highest casualties (428 killed, 298 injured), driven by intercommunal cattle raiding and politically orchestrated militia violence. The escalation is directly linked to the political crisis surrounding the scheduled national elections (repeatedly postponed, now targeted for December 2026) and the fragmentation of the unified forces envisioned by R-ARCSS. Q2 and Q3 2024 also saw surges: UNMISS recorded 317 incidents in Q2 2024 alone, affecting 1,062 civilians. South Sudan does not publish official homicide statistics — all figures derive from UNMISS human rights monitoring.

Corruption & Accountability

South Sudan scores 8 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — the worst score of any country in the world, ranking 180th out of 180 countries, below Somalia (9), Venezuela (10), and Syria (12). The CPI 2025 (February 2026) shows a marginal improvement to 9/100, rank 181/182, as the index expanded — still the second worst globally. South Sudan has held the bottom CPI position or near-bottom every year since its first assessment in 2012. Corruption is not merely endemic but constitutive of the state structure: oil revenues — which represent over 95% of government income — are systematically diverted through parallel procurement networks, ghost soldier payrolls (an estimated 40% of SSPDF payroll is fraudulent), and direct extraction by political and military elites. The IMF estimated in a 2024 Article IV consultation that South Sudan loses over USD 1.5 billion annually to corrupt diversion — in a country where per capita income is under USD 500.

South Sudan's accountability landscape is defined by near-total impunity. The Hybrid Court for South Sudan (HCSS) — mandated under Chapter V of the R-ARCSS to prosecute war crimes and crimes against humanity committed since 2013 — had still not been operationalised as of May 2026, having been blocked by President Kiir's government for over seven years. The African Union Commission, which is responsible for establishing the HCSS, signed a Memorandum of Understanding with the South Sudanese transitional government in October 2024 — a procedural step, but not yet a functional court. The most significant domestic accountability proceeding is the ongoing trial of former First Vice President Riek Machar and 24 co-accused for treason and related offences — a politically motivated prosecution, according to Human Rights Watch (June 2025), that bears the hallmarks of judicial capture rather than genuine accountability. The Sudd Institute and the Africa Centre for Strategic Studies have repeatedly identified South Sudan as the most significant test case for the African Union's transitional justice commitments.

Institutional Landmark
The Judiciary of South Sudan — Juba

Le portail de la juridiction de Juba — siège de la Cour suprême du Soudan du Sud, du tribunal pour violences basées sur le genre et du tribunal pour mineurs — symbolise les ambitions institutionnelles du plus jeune État du monde depuis son indépendance en 2011. Derrière cette porte, moins de 1% du budget national finance l'ensemble du système judiciaire d'un pays de 12 millions d'habitants traversé par des décennies de guerre civile. En 2024, le Comité de réforme judiciaire soutenu par l'UE a validé 15 recommandations critiques pour reconstruire une justice indépendante — une réforme dont la mise en œuvre demeure partielle en mai 2026.
