Judicial Independence Crisis

Mexico

Estados Unidos Mexicanos Civil Law — Latin America

The Judicial Framework

Mexico operates a civil law system under the Political Constitution of 1917 — one of the first constitutions in the world to enshrine social rights, preceding the Weimar Constitution by two years. The federal judiciary has traditionally been structured across Juzgados de Distrito (district courts), Tribunales Colegiados de Circuito (collegiate circuit courts), Tribunales Unitarios de Circuito, the Tribunal Electoral del Poder Judicial de la Federación (TEPJF), and at the apex, the Suprema Corte de Justicia de la Nación (SCJN) in Mexico City's Centro Histórico — an eleven-member body whose justices were historically appointed by the President with Senate confirmation and served 15-year non-renewable terms. The SCJN, housed in the art deco-neoclassical building on Pino Suárez facing the Zócalo, has two permanent chambers and exercises both constitutional review and final appellate jurisdiction.

Mexico became the first country in the world to require popular election of all judges at every level — from district courts to the Supreme Court — following President Claudia Sheinbaum's first major constitutional reform, passed by a two-thirds Senate supermajority on 11 September 2024. The reform reduces the SCJN from 11 to 9 justices, shortens terms to 12 years, eliminates minimum age and experience requirements, and mandates the election of over 6,500 judges, magistrates, and ministers. Judicial elections were held in June 2025 to fill a majority of positions nationwide — the first such elections in Mexican history — prompting the resignation of six of eleven sitting SCJN justices and mass demonstrations by the legal community and international observers including the UN Special Rapporteur on the Independence of Judges. Human Rights Watch reported in September 2025 that the electoral process severely undermined judicial independence, with candidates backed by Morena and cartel-linked networks winning key positions in high-crime states.

Mexico recorded 29,675 intentional homicides in 2023 — a rate of approximately 24 per 100,000 population and a 4.18% decline from 2022 — making it one of the most violent countries in the world outside active conflict zones. In the first half of 2024, 15,243 murders were registered — a 4.7% further decline — with INEGI reporting a rate of 11.7 per 100,000 for that semester alone. Firearms accounted for 73% of homicides in H1 2024. Mexico's homicide crisis is geographically concentrated: Guanajuato, Baja California, Chihuahua, Jalisco, and Estado de México together account for over 40% of all murders, driven by territorial conflicts between criminal organisations including the Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG), the Sinaloa Cartel, and the Carteles Unidos. Organised crime-related killings represent an estimated 70–80% of all Mexican homicides, though official attribution statistics remain contested.

Corruption & Accountability

Mexico scores 26 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — its worst score in the history of the index, plummeting from 31/100 (rank 126) in 2023 to 26/100 (rank 140) in 2024, a five-point collapse representing the steepest single-year decline of any country in Latin America. Baker McKenzie's analysis attributes the catastrophic deterioration to the September 2024 judicial overhaul — which directly undermined the institutional independence of the judiciary — combined with the expansion of executive power, the elimination of autonomous regulatory agencies, and accelerating impunity for organised crime. Transparency International Mexico describes the 2024 score as "an institutional alarm signal of the first order." The CPI 2025 (TI, published February 2026) shows a marginal recovery to 27/100, rank 141 — statistically insignificant.

Mexico's anti-corruption architecture — formally one of the most elaborate in Latin America, comprising the Sistema Nacional Anticorrupción (SNA), the Fiscalía General de la República (FGR), the Auditoría Superior de la Federación (ASF), and the Secretaría de la Función Pública (SFP) — has been progressively hollowed out since 2018. Under the AMLO presidency (2018–2024), 13 of 17 autonomous constitutional bodies were dissolved or merged by referendum or executive decree — including the IFETEL (telecoms regulator), COFECE (competition authority), INAI (transparency and data protection agency), and CONEVAL (poverty measurement body). Their elimination removed the independent watchdog layer that previously checked executive corruption. Structural impunity persists: Mexico's conviction rate for homicides stands at approximately 8% — meaning 9 of every 10 murders result in no conviction — and its conviction rate for corruption offences is even lower.

Institutional Landmark
Suprema Corte de Justicia de la Nación — Mexico City

La Suprema Corte de Justicia de la Nación, inaugurée en 1941 sur l'ancien emplacement du Palacio de los Betlemitas face au Zócalo de la Ciudad de México, est le plus haut tribunal du pays. Sa façade art déco en pierre volcanique, avec son grand drapeau mexicain en façade et ses murales intérieurs de José Clemente Orozco et Diego Rivera, symbolise la souveraineté constitutionnelle héritée de la Révolution de 1910. En septembre 2024, pour la première fois dans l'histoire de toute démocratie, ses juges ont été soumis à l'élection populaire directe — une réforme qui a provoqué la démission de six de ses onze ministres et une crise internationale.
