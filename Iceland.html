Iceland

The Judicial Framework

Iceland's legal system is rooted in Nordic civil law, shaped by centuries of customary law codified through the Althing — the world's oldest functioning parliament, established in 930 AD. The judicial structure consists of district courts (héraðsdómstólar), the Court of Appeal (Landsréttur), established in 2018, and the Supreme Court (Hæstiréttur Íslands), which serves as the final court of cassation. The 2018 judicial reform, which introduced the intermediate appellate tier, was specifically designed to reduce the Supreme Court's workload and ensure more thorough review of criminal and civil cases.

Iceland's criminal justice system is founded on principles of restorative justice and rehabilitation. Research published in 2025 highlights the growing role of restorative justice practices in Icelandic prisons, including victim-offender mediation and community conferencing, as complementary mechanisms alongside traditional sentencing. The system emphasises reintegration, with short prison terms, open prisons, and extensive use of community service and conditional sentences for non-violent offenders.

Iceland consistently records one of the world's lowest homicide rates — typically below 1 per 100,000 population. UNODC data confirms that Iceland reported zero firearm homicides in 2023, a reflection of the country's strict gun laws, high social trust, low inequality, and strong community cohesion. The World Bank Rule of Law Index 2024 scores Iceland at 1.63 out of 2.5, consistent with its long-standing status as one of the world's most law-abiding societies.

Corruption & Accountability

Iceland scores 77 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 10th globally — unchanged from 2023 and a significant recovery from its post-financial crisis low. The 2008 banking collapse exposed serious governance failures and led to landmark prosecutions of bank executives and politicians, fundamentally reshaping Iceland's approach to financial regulation and institutional accountability.

Iceland's anti-corruption framework is reinforced by a strong tradition of press freedom, high public sector transparency, and active civil society. The country has consistently ranked among the top nations in the World Press Freedom Index, which serves as a critical check on public and political accountability. The Special Prosecutor's Office (Sérstakur saksóknari), established in the aftermath of the 2008 crisis, successfully prosecuted numerous cases of financial misconduct and remains a key institution for holding powerful actors accountable.
