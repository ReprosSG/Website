Belgium

Kingdom of Belgium Western European Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

Belgium operates a civil law system rooted in the Napoleonic Code (1804), reflecting the country's historic position at the crossroads of French and Germanic legal traditions. The Belgian judicial hierarchy is organised into five levels: Justices of the Peace (Justice de paix / Vredegerecht) and Police Courts at the base, followed by Courts of First Instance (Tribunal de première instance), Courts of Appeal (Cour d'appel) in five judicial districts (Brussels, Liège, Ghent, Antwerp, and Mons), and at the apex, the Court of Cassation (Cour de cassation / Hof van Cassatie) — the supreme court for all civil, criminal, and commercial matters. Alongside this hierarchy, the Constitutional Court (Cour constitutionnelle) exercises exclusive jurisdiction over constitutional review, while the Council of State (Conseil d'État) adjudicates administrative disputes.

Belgium's federal structure — comprising three linguistic communities (French, Flemish, and German-speaking) and three regions (Wallonia, Flanders, and Brussels-Capital) — creates a uniquely complex legal landscape where legislative competences are distributed across multiple levels of government. This institutional complexity is both a hallmark of Belgian democratic pluralism and a recognised source of structural inefficiency: the European Commission's 2024 Rule of Law Report notes persistent challenges including backlogs in courts, under-resourcing of the judiciary relative to European peers, and the need to accelerate digitalisation of court proceedings. Belgium has made targeted investments through its "Justice Plan" reform programme (2019–2024) to modernise procedural law and reduce caseload pressures.

Belgium records a national homicide rate of 1.38 per 100,000 population (2023, Eurostat) — the third highest in the European Union after Latvia and Lithuania, a figure driven significantly by the Brussels-Capital Region, which recorded 3.19 homicides per 100,000 in 2023 — the second highest of any major EU region. The Brussels rate is strongly correlated with drug trafficking networks operating through the Port of Antwerp, one of Europe's principal entry points for cocaine, and with related gang violence concentrated in specific urban neighbourhoods. Flanders recorded a substantially lower rate of 0.80 per 100,000, while Wallonia stood at 1.84. These stark regional disparities illustrate the deeply uneven geography of public safety within the Belgian federation.

Corruption & Accountability

Belgium scores 69 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 22nd globally — a four-point drop from 73 in 2023 and a sustained decline from its peak of 77 in 2016. Transparency International Belgium notes that this places the country below its direct neighbours Germany (16th, 75), the Netherlands (9th, 83), and Luxembourg (5th, 80), while remaining ahead of France (25th, 68). Marc Beyens, Executive Director of TI Belgium, stated the results represent "a missed opportunity" and called for binding reforms to public procurement, asset declarations for officials, and the reduction of undue business influence on political decision-making.

Belgium's anti-corruption institutional architecture includes the Central Office for the Repression of Corruption (OCRC / CDBC), embedded within the Federal Judicial Police, and the Court of Audit (Cour des comptes / Rekenhof), which exercises independent financial oversight of public accounts. Belgium has faced recurring high-profile corruption episodes: the "Kazakhgate" affair (2020–2024), involving alleged illegal parliamentary lobbying on behalf of Kazakh business interests, and the "Qatargate" European Parliament scandal (2022–2024), which implicated Belgian MEPs and NGOs in a cash-for-influence scheme, have both significantly damaged public trust in Belgian and European political institutions. The European Commission's 2024 Rule of Law Report urged Belgium to strengthen its anti-money laundering framework and accelerate implementation of lobbying transparency legislation.

Institutional Landmark
Palais de Justice — Brussels, Belgium

The Palais de Justice de Bruxelles, designed by architect Joseph Poelaert and inaugurated in 1883, is one of the largest buildings constructed in the 19th century and remains the biggest courthouse in the world by volume. Its monumental neo-eclectic dome dominates the Brussels skyline and houses the Court of Cassation, the Courts of Appeal, and the Court of First Instance — embodying the ambition of a newly independent Belgium to enshrine justice at the heart of its national identity.
