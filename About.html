The Project

Justice World is a data-driven web platform built as part of the Web Programming course. Its purpose is to raise awareness around UN Sustainable Development Goal 16 (SDG 16) — Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions — by visualising global data on corruption, homicide rates, and rule of law in an accessible and interactive format.

The platform presents country-level profiles, a global CPI rankings table, and an interactive world map, all built from a blank page using HTML, CSS (Tailwind CSS), and JavaScript — with no back-end framework or database. The visual design system was established with the assistance of an AI tool and served as a foundation for all pages.

Methodology

All data presented on Justice World is sourced from internationally recognised and publicly available institutions. No data has been modified, extrapolated, or estimated beyond what is published in the original reports.

Corruption scores are drawn from the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI) 2024, which aggregates data from 13 independent sources to produce a score on a scale of 0 (highly corrupt) to 100 (very clean) for 180 countries.

Homicide rates are sourced from the United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) Global Study on Homicide and national statistics agencies (e.g. Statistics Canada, NZ Police), expressed as intentional homicides per 100,000 population.

Rule of law scores are taken from the World Justice Project Rule of Law Index 2024, which evaluates 142 countries across eight dimensions including constraints on government powers, absence of corruption, open government, and civil and criminal justice.

Limitations

The CPI is a perception-based index — it reflects how corruption is perceived by experts and business leaders, not necessarily the actual level of corruption. Countries with strong media environments and high transparency may score better simply because their corruption is more visible and documented.

Homicide data reporting quality varies significantly across countries. In nations with weak institutional capacity or ongoing conflict, under-reporting is common, meaning official figures may underestimate the true rate.

The country profiles currently available represent a non-exhaustive selection of countries, prioritising those at the top of the CPI ranking. The platform is under active development; additional country pages are planned.
