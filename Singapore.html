Singapore

Regional Hub Asia-Pacific Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

Singapore's legal system is built on a common law tradition inherited from the United Kingdom, combined with a highly centralised administrative state and a judiciary known for procedural efficiency. In the World Justice Project Rule of Law Index 2024, Singapore ranks 16th out of 142 countries worldwide and 4th in East Asia and the Pacific, reflecting strong performance in order, regulatory enforcement, and absence of corruption.

In 2024, Chief Justice Sundaresh Menon argued that Singapore's conception of the rule of law is being renewed to focus more explicitly on accessibility, proportionality, and coherence. This approach is reflected in procedural reforms such as simplified proceedings in the State Courts, which are designed to reduce cost and complexity for court users and improve practical access to justice.

Singapore's criminal justice system remains firm on deterrence, especially for serious offences, but it has evolved over time. Amendments introduced in 2012 shifted some offences away from a fully mandatory death penalty model to a more discretionary system, showing a calibrated balance between deterrence, judicial safeguards, and the administration of justice.

Corruption & Accountability

Singapore ranks 3rd globally in the 2024 Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index with a score of 84 out of 100, making it the least corrupt country in the Asia-Pacific region. The Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau attributes this performance to consistent anti-corruption enforcement, strong public sector controls, and a political culture that treats integrity as a core state function.

The city-state's reputation for clean government is reinforced by powerful oversight institutions, strict procurement rules, and low tolerance for bribery in public administration. At the same time, Singapore continues to debate how rule of law, efficiency, and civil liberties should be balanced within a tightly managed legal and political framework.

In public safety terms, Singapore also records one of the world's lowest homicide rates. A 2024 Ministry of Law statistical handout citing UNODC data reports an intentional homicide rate of 0.12 per 100,000 population for 2022, underscoring the country's exceptionally low level of lethal violence.

Institutional Landmark
Old Supreme Court Building, Singapore

Built in 1939, the neoclassical Old Supreme Court Building served as Singapore's highest court until 2005. Its iconic green dome and Corinthian columns make it one of Singapore's most recognisable heritage buildings.
