Party-Controlled Judiciary

China

中华人民共和国 Socialist Law — East Asia

The Judicial Framework

China operates a socialist law system rooted in the civil law tradition, but fundamentally distinct from any liberal legal order: the judiciary operates under the explicit leadership of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP), enshrined in the 2018 constitutional amendment which added "Party leadership" as the most fundamental feature of socialism with Chinese characteristics. The judicial hierarchy runs from Basic People's Courts (base level) through Intermediate People's Courts, Higher People's Courts (one per province/autonomous region/municipality), to the Supreme People's Court (最高人民法院 — SPC) in Beijing — which issues judicial interpretations binding on all lower courts. The Supreme People's Procuratorate (最高人民检察院) serves as the parallel prosecutorial apex. China's courts are formally independent under Article 126 of the Constitution, but in practice are supervised by the Political-Legal Affairs Committees of the CCP at every level — ensuring Party direction over adjudication in politically sensitive cases.

In December 2024, the SPC issued its landmark Sixth Five-Year Reform Outline of the People's Courts (2024–2028) — the most comprehensive judicial reform blueprint since the Fifth Outline (2019–2023). The plan prioritises three pillars: deepening digitalisation of court proceedings (China already leads the world in online adjudication, with over 3.7 million cases handled online in 2024), strengthening the "Three Supremes" principle (the supremacy of Party authority, constitution, and people's interests), and reforming the intellectual property court system. The SPC's 2024 Annual Work Report confirmed that people's courts across China concluded 35.26 million cases in 2024 — the highest annual caseload in history — with a clearance rate exceeding 98.3%. Serious violent crime cases (intentional homicide, robbery, kidnapping, arson) fell below 16,000 in 2024 — a historic low — down from over 40,000 in 2015, according to a November 2025 SPC national conference on criminal trials.

China recorded a homicide rate of 0.44 per 100,000 population in 2024 — officially one of the lowest in the world, confirmed by Political Commissar Jiang Guoli of the Ministry of Public Security's Criminal Investigation Bureau at a July 2025 State Council press conference. Total criminal cases filed in 2024 fell 25.7% year-on-year, the sharpest annual decline since the Ministry of Public Security began publishing statistics. China's extraordinarily low official homicide rate — roughly 0.46/100k in 2023 (UNODC-cited figure) — is attributed to pervasive surveillance infrastructure (estimated 700 million CCTV cameras as of 2024), near-universal digital ID and facial recognition deployment, strict gun control (civilian firearm ownership is effectively prohibited), and rapid police response enabled by the world's largest policing apparatus. Independent verification of Chinese crime statistics is structurally impossible given the absence of press freedom and judicial transparency.

Corruption & Accountability

China scores 43 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — unchanged from 43 in 2023, ranking 76th globally. This score places China above the global average (43) and ahead of Brazil (34), India (38), and South Africa (41) among BRICS nations — but significantly below the Asia-Pacific average and light years from the transparency scores of Singapore (84), Hong Kong (74), or Japan (71). The stagnation of China's score at 43 for multiple consecutive years reflects the paradox of Xi Jinping's anti-corruption campaign: the most extensive anti-corruption drive in the history of the People's Republic — having investigated over 4.7 million officials since 2012, including 553 "tigers" (senior officials above vice-minister level) — has not translated into improved perceived transparency, because the campaign is conducted by Party discipline mechanisms rather than independent courts, and is widely perceived as a political purge tool rather than a structural anti-corruption reform.

China's Central Commission for Discipline Inspection (CCDI) — the Party's primary anti-corruption organ — reported investigating 714,000 officials and imposing 621,000 disciplinary actions in 2024 alone. Notable 2024–2025 cases include the prosecution of former Defence Ministers Li Shangfu and Wei Fenghe for bribery (both expelled from the Party in 2024 and sentenced in 2025), and the ongoing investigation of officials connected to the Rocket Force corruption scandal which triggered the largest military leadership purge since the Cultural Revolution. China's anti-corruption framework has no independent oversight: the CCDI, National Supervisory Commission, and People's Procuratorate all operate under direct CCP leadership. Transparency International's Asia-Pacific 2024 analysis notes that "without rule of law, judicial independence, or press freedom, anti-corruption efforts in China remain instruments of political control rather than genuine institutional reform."

Institutional Landmark
最高人民法院 — Supreme People's Court, Beijing

La Cour suprême du peuple (最高人民法院), siège sur l'ancienne avenue Chang'an à Pékin, dans le quartier historique des légations. Sa façade de style sino-soviétique — arche centrale en pierre, lions gardiens en bronze, drapeau national chinois — reflète l'architecture institutionnelle de la République populaire depuis 1954. La cour a rendu 35.26 millions de décisions en 2024 — le plus grand volume annuel de l'histoire judiciaire mondiale — et a publié le 6e Plan quinquennal de réforme judiciaire, orientant la modernisation des tribunaux chinois jusqu'en 2028.
