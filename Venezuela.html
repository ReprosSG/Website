Venezuela

República Bolivariana de Venezuela Civil Law — Latin America

The Judicial Framework

Venezuela operates a civil law system under the Constitution of 1999 — drafted and adopted during Hugo Chávez's first year in power — which created the Tribunal Supremo de Justicia (TSJ) as the apex of an integrated judicial system theoretically independent of the other branches of government. The TSJ comprises six specialised chambers: Constitutional, Political-Administrative, Electoral, Civil Cassation, Criminal Cassation, and Social Cassation. Beneath the TSJ, the structure includes Superior Courts, Courts of First Instance, and Municipal Courts, all nominally coordinated by the Dirección Ejecutiva de la Magistratura (DEM). The current TSJ president is Caryslia Rodríguez, appointed under President Maduro and serving simultaneously as president of the Electoral Chamber — a structural concentration of electoral judicial and institutional power in a single loyalist figure that has been widely condemned by international legal experts.

Venezuela's TSJ became internationally notorious on 22 August 2024 when, without publishing detailed voting records or conducting an independent audit, it ratified Nicolás Maduro's claimed victory in the July 28, 2024 presidential election — a result that the United States, European Union, most Latin American governments, and the Carter Center rejected as fraudulent based on opposition tally data showing Edmundo González winning with over 60% of the vote. The TSJ ruling declared that the evidence "unquestionably certifies" a Maduro victory, and ordered the Attorney General to investigate González for electoral fraud — a legal manoeuvre that criminalised the principal opposition candidate and forced him into exile in Spain. The ruling was described by the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights (IACHR) as "an instrument of political persecution incompatible with the minimum standards of judicial independence." In January 2026, the TSJ inaugurated its judicial year 2026 presenting the 2025 management report — a ceremony broadcast by state television teleSUR framing the court as a pillar of the Bolivarian state.

Venezuela recorded a homicide rate of 26.2 per 100,000 population in 2024 — the fifth highest in Latin America — down from 26.8 in 2023 and a peak of approximately 90/100k in 2015–2016, according to Statista citing OVV (Venezuelan Violence Observatory) data. The OVV's 2023 annual report documented 6,973 violent deaths, of which 1,956 were homicides by criminal actors, 953 were deaths from resistance to authority (EFCI — killings by police and security forces), and 3,448 were under investigation. EFCI killings — extrajudicial executions conducted by the Fuerza de Acciones Especiales (FAES) and other security forces — have been extensively documented by the UN Human Rights Office as potential crimes against humanity: a 2024 OHCHR update confirmed that the FAES continued operating in low-income urban sectors, with summary executions recorded in Caracas, Carabobo, and Miranda states. Venezuela's homicide rate, while improving from its catastrophic 2015 peak, remains approximately fourteen times higher than the Western European average.

Corruption & Accountability

Venezuela scores 10 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — unchanged from 10 in 2023 — ranking 180th out of 182 countries, placing it among the three most corrupt nations on earth alongside South Sudan (8) and Somalia (9). The CPI 2025 (TI, February 2026) maintained the score at 10/100, rank 180/182. Venezuela has held one of the three lowest CPI scores in the world continuously since 2017, reflecting the complete capture of state institutions — judiciary, prosecution, regulatory bodies, oil company PDVSA, the central bank, and the customs authority — by networks loyal to President Maduro and the military-civilian Bolivarian elite. Venezuela's economy has contracted by approximately 80% in real terms since 2013, generating the largest forced migration crisis in Latin American history: over 7.7 million Venezuelans had fled the country by the end of 2024, according to UNHCR — the second largest displacement crisis globally after Ukraine.

Venezuela's most significant corruption case on the international stage remains the PDVSA network: the United States Department of Justice has charged over 40 Venezuelan officials, military officers, businessmen, and intermediaries with laundering over USD 1.2 billion diverted from state oil company Petróleos de Venezuela (PDVSA) through US financial institutions. In June 2024, former PDVSA treasurer Nervis Villalobos was sentenced to nine years in US federal prison for laundering $160 million. The Maduro government has made no domestic prosecutions of PDVSA corruption since 2019 — despite the collapse of oil production from 3.2 million barrels/day in 2000 to under 800,000 in 2024. International accountability mechanisms — the ICC (which opened a formal investigation into Venezuela in 2021, the first in Latin American history), IACHR, and US and EU targeted sanctions covering over 200 Venezuelan officials — represent the primary accountability mechanisms, given the complete absence of independent domestic oversight.

Institutional Landmark
Palacio de Justicia — Avenida Bolívar, Caracas

Le Palacio de Justicia de Caracas, conçu par l'architecte Carlos Raúl Villanueva et inauguré en 1962, est le siège historique de la justice vénézuélienne sur l'Avenida Bolívar — l'axe monumental de la capitale. Son imposante arche brutaliste en béton et sa sculpture centrale symbolisent la justice de la República — une institution progressivement vidée de son indépendance sous les gouvernements Chávez puis Maduro. En août 2024, le Tribunal Supremo de Justicia, dont les magistrats sont tous nommés par le régime, a validé la réélection contestée de Maduro, transformant la cour suprême en instrument de légitimation électorale.
