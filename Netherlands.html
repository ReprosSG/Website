Netherlands

Regional Hub Western European Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

The Netherlands operates under a civil law system rooted in the Napoleonic tradition, with a layered judicial structure: district courts (rechtbanken), courts of appeal (gerechtshoven), and the Supreme Court (Hoge Raad der Nederlanden), seated in The Hague. The Supreme Court serves as the final court of cassation, ensuring uniform application of national law. The Netherlands is also uniquely home to key international judicial institutions, including the International Court of Justice (ICJ), the International Criminal Court (ICC), and the Permanent Court of Arbitration — making The Hague the undisputed global capital of international justice.

The Dutch criminal justice system is built on the principles of proportionality, subsidiarity, and rehabilitation. Public prosecutors (officieren van justitie) operate under the Public Prosecution Service (Openbaar Ministerie), which has broad discretion in deciding whether to prosecute, divert, or settle cases through out-of-court arrangements. A 2024 State Commission on the Rule of Law report acknowledged that while institutions remain strong, access to justice has been compromised for many citizens by legal aid funding cuts and a chronic capacity shortage in the prison system.

The Netherlands recorded 125 intentional homicides in 2023, a rate of 0.69 per 100,000 population — a 13.5% decrease from 2022, reflecting the effectiveness of targeted organised crime interventions and increased cooperation between police, prosecution, and intelligence services (UNODC / CBS, 2023). The government has significantly reinforced its approach to dismantling drug trafficking networks, particularly through the Multidisciplinary Intervention Team (MIT) launched in 2019.

Corruption & Accountability

The Netherlands scores 78 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 8th globally — unchanged from the previous year. While the score remains high by global standards, Transparency International's Western Europe report flags concerns about the Netherlands' vulnerability to illicit financial flows through its role as a major European financial and logistics hub, and highlights unresolved weaknesses in lobbying regulation and the revolving door between the private sector and politics.

The Dutch anti-corruption framework is anchored by the General Integrity Bureau (Bureau Integriteitsbevordering Openbare Sector, BIOS), the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU-Nederland), and the National Prosecutor's Office for Serious Fraud, Environmental Crime and Asset Confiscation (Functioneel Parket). The Netherlands has been a consistent advocate for EU-wide anti-corruption measures and participates actively in GRECO (Group of States Against Corruption) peer review cycles, which have recommended strengthened whistleblower protections and more transparent party financing rules.

Institutional Landmark
Peace Palace (Vredespaleis) — The Hague

Built in 1913, the Peace Palace in The Hague is home to the International Court of Justice and the Permanent Court of Arbitration. It stands as the world's most iconic symbol of international law and peaceful dispute resolution.
