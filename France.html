France

République Française Civil Law — Western Europe

The Judicial Framework

France's legal system is the archetype of the Napoleonic civil law tradition — the model upon which the legal systems of over 70 countries are based. The judiciary is divided into two entirely separate orders: the judicial order (ordre judiciaire), headed by the Cour de cassation, handles civil and criminal matters; and the administrative order (ordre administratif), headed by the Conseil d'État, reviews all acts of the executive and public authorities. This strict separation between judicial and administrative law is a defining feature of the French constitutional model, reflecting an 18th-century republican principle that courts must not interfere with government administration. A Tribunal des conflits arbitrates jurisdictional disputes between the two orders.

At the apex of France's constitutional architecture sits the Conseil constitutionnel, composed of nine members appointed for non-renewable nine-year terms — three each by the President of the Republic, the President of the National Assembly, and the President of the Senate. In February 2025, Senator Muriel Jourda introduced a landmark constitutional bill (PPL constitutionnelle n°354) to reform the Conseil, requiring all nominees to hold recognised legal expertise, imposing a three-fifths parliamentary supermajority for their confirmation, and abolishing the controversial status of "member for life" enjoyed by former Presidents of the Republic. The reform responds to repeated criticism that politically motivated nominations — most recently the appointment of Jacqueline Gourault in 2022 — undermine the Conseil's perceived independence. As of May 2026, the bill remains under parliamentary examination.

France recorded 976 criminal homicides in 2024 — a rate of approximately 1.42 per 100,000 population — marking a 2% decline for the first time since 2020, according to the Ministry of the Interior's SSMSI annual crime statistics bulletin published in July 2025. This reverses the sustained upward trend observed since 2016, when 926 homicides were recorded (+5.4% cumulative over eight years). Attempted homicides, however, continued to surge: +7% in 2024, maintaining an average annual increase of +8% since 2016. France's homicide rate remains well below the EU average of approximately 2.5 per 100,000 and is comparable to Germany (0.9) and Italy (0.6), though higher than similarly-sized northern European democracies.

Corruption & Accountability

France scores 66 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 27th globally out of 182 countries — a one-point decline from 67 in 2023 and a drop of two places from 25th. Connexion France notes this places France behind the United Kingdom (71, 20th), Germany (75, 15th), and many other European democracies, but ahead of the United States (65, 29th). Transparency International's Western Europe and EU report for 2024 attributes the region's worsening performance — including France's — to "leaders' hollow efforts" in fighting corruption, identifying persistent lobbying opacity, revolving-door practices between the public and private sectors, and insufficient protection of whistleblowers as structural weaknesses.

France's principal anti-corruption institutions include the Agence française anticorruption (AFA), created in 2016 under the Sapin II law, which advises public and private entities on compliance and conducts mandatory audits; and the Parquet national financier (PNF), a specialised financial prosecution unit established in 2014 that has led landmark investigations including the Bygmalion affair and multiple cases involving political party financing. The Haute autorité pour la transparence de la vie publique (HATVP) manages mandatory asset and interest declarations for elected officials and senior civil servants. Despite these institutions, high-profile corruption scandals continue to emerge regularly — including the 2024 MaPrimeRénov' fraud revelations involving €1.5 billion in fraudulent energy renovation subsidies — reflecting persistent vulnerabilities in public procurement and subsidy allocation.

Institutional Landmark
Conseil constitutionnel — Palais-Royal, Paris

Since 1959, the Constitutional Council has been housed in the Montpensier Wing of the Palais-Royal, a 17th-century building constructed under Richelieu and expanded under Louis XIV. Its nine members—known as the “Sages”—have ensured that laws comply with the Constitution of the Fifth Republic since 1958, and, following the 2010 reform of the priority preliminary ruling on constitutionality (QPC), any litigant may refer a matter to them to challenge the constitutionality of a legislative provision applicable to their case.
