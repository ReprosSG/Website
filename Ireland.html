The Judicial Framework

Ireland operates under a common law system derived from English law, with a written constitution — Bunreacht na hÉireann (1937) — that enshrines fundamental rights and establishes the separation of powers. The judicial hierarchy consists of the District Court, the Circuit Court, the High Court, the Court of Appeal, and the Supreme Court, which is the final arbiter of constitutional questions. The Four Courts complex in Dublin, designed in the 18th century by James Gandon, remains the historic seat of the superior courts and one of Ireland's most recognised architectural landmarks.

The Irish criminal justice system is adversarial in nature, with a strong emphasis on the presumption of innocence, the right to a fair trial, and judicial independence. Ireland has undertaken significant legislative reforms in recent years, including the Criminal Justice (Hate Offences) Act 2024, the Criminal Justice (Sexual Offences and Human Trafficking) Act 2024, and the Policing, Security and Community Safety Act 2024 — the latter fundamentally restructuring the oversight of An Garda Síochána (the national police). The EU Rule of Law Report 2024 notes that judicial independence in Ireland remains high and that the new judicial appointments commission has strengthened the merit-based selection of judges.

Ireland recorded 34 intentional homicides in 2023, representing a rate of 0.65 per 100,000 population — a significant decrease from 44 cases and 0.86 per 100,000 in 2022, reaching one of its lowest levels in over two decades. This decline reflects successful targeted policing operations, particularly against organised crime networks, and improved cooperation between An Garda Síochána and European law enforcement agencies through Europol and Eurojust.

Corruption & Accountability

Ireland scores 77 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 12th globally — a one-point decline from 2023 but still firmly in the top tier. The country's strong performance reflects robust judicial independence, a free press, and effective public administration oversight mechanisms including the Standards in Public Office Commission (SIPO) and the Office of the Comptroller and Auditor General.

Transparency International Ireland has flagged ongoing concerns about the pace of reform in lobbying regulation and corporate political donations, as well as the need for a stronger statutory framework for whistleblower protection in the private sector. Ireland's role as a major European headquarters for multinational corporations also raises scrutiny around transfer pricing practices and tax arrangements, areas where international pressure through the OECD BEPS framework has prompted legislative adjustments including the introduction of a 15% global minimum corporate tax rate in 2024.
