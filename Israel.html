The Judicial Framework

Israel's legal system is a unique hybrid of English common law, Ottoman legal tradition, Jewish religious law (Halakha), and modern civil legislation enacted since independence in 1948. Uniquely among democracies, Israel has no written constitution — instead, governance is structured around a series of Basic Laws (חוקי יסוד) that function as quasi-constitutional instruments. The judiciary comprises Magistrates' Courts, District Courts in six districts, and the Supreme Court (בית המשפט העליון, Beit HaMishpat HaElyon) in Jerusalem, which functions both as a court of final appeal and, crucially, as the High Court of Justice (Bagatz) — a unique institution empowered to hear original petitions directly from any citizen challenging governmental actions without any right-of-standing threshold.

The Supreme Court's independence remained the defining constitutional battleground through 2025. After the Supreme Court struck down the "reasonableness" law in January 2024, the Netanyahu coalition relaunched the judicial overhaul: in March 2025, the Knesset passed a new Judicial Appointments Committee law by 67–1 (the opposition boycotting en masse), restructuring the nine-member committee to give politicians greater control over judicial appointments. In January 2025, Justice Esther Hayut's 16-month vacancy as Supreme Court President was filled by Isaac Amit — but Justice Minister Yariv Levin refused to recognise Amit's authority and boycotted his swearing-in ceremony, an unprecedented act of institutional defiance. As of December 2025, all eleven Supreme Court justices are scheduled to hear the constitutional challenge to the new law in June 2026.

Israel recorded 295 homicides in 2025 — a national rate of approximately 3.2 per 100,000 population, double the 147 recorded in 2020. 2025 was the deadliest year ever recorded for Arab society in Israel: 252 Arab citizens were killed in 218 separate violent incidents, compared to 230 in 2024 and 28 Jewish victims — an Arab:Jewish homicide ratio of 14:1 (Taub Center, 2025). In 2026, the trend continues unabated: over 104 people have been murdered since January 1st. The root causes identified by researchers include the proliferation of illegal firearms in northern and coastal districts, entrenched clan-based criminal networks, and structural under-policing of Arab municipalities — a crisis that successive governments have failed to resolve despite repeated public commitments.

Corruption & Accountability

Israel scores 62 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2025 (published February 2026), ranking 35th globally — a drop of two points and five places from its 2024 position (64, 30th). israel.com notes the decline reflects deepening public concerns about the erosion of institutional checks following the 2025 judicial overhaul and the Netanyahu corruption trial, which entered its sixth year in 2025. The trial — covering three separate cases of bribery, fraud and breach of trust — continued amid the Gaza war, fuelling public debate about the compatibility of leading a wartime government while standing as a criminal defendant.

Israel's anti-corruption enforcement is anchored by the State Comptroller's Office (מבקר המדינה) and the Police's National Fraud Investigation Unit (Lahav 433). The Netanyahu trial — now entering its sixth year — continued through 2025, with the Prime Minister accused of receiving benefits from media moguls and telecom executives in exchange for regulatory favours. The simultaneous push to restructure judicial appointments has generated an unprecedented conflict-of-interest perception: the March 2025 Judicial Appointments Committee law was condemned by the Israeli Bar Association, the opposition, and international observers as a mechanism to ultimately influence the courts adjudicating Netanyahu's own case. The Supreme Court's hearing on the law's constitutionality, scheduled for June 2026, is widely regarded as the most consequential legal proceeding in Israel's history.

Institutional Landmark
בית המשפט העליון — Supreme Court of Israel, Jerusalem

The Supreme Court of Israel, inaugurated in 1992 and designed by architects Ada Karmi-Melamede and Ram Karmi, is a landmark of modern Israeli architecture. Built from Jerusalem stone, it blends contemporary design with traditional Middle Eastern architectural motifs. The building's symbolic use of light — a circular skylight traversing the courthouse's axis — is a deliberate architectural statement about the relationship between justice and enlightenment in the Jewish democratic state.
