The Judicial Framework

Syria's legal system is a hybrid of French civil law tradition inherited from the French Mandate period (1920–1946) and Islamic law (sharia) applied in personal status matters for Muslim citizens, with distinct rules for Christian and Jewish communities under Ottoman-era communal law frameworks. Prior to December 2024, the formal judicial structure was headed by the Supreme Judicial Council under the Ba'athist state, with the Supreme Constitutional Court serving as the apex institution for constitutional review — both institutions entirely subordinate to the Assad regime's security apparatus and serving primarily as instruments of authoritarian control and political repression. The Sednaya Military Prison — documented by Amnesty International in its 2017 report "Human Slaughterhouse" — was the most visible expression of the judicial system's role as an engine of mass atrocity: an estimated 13,000 people were executed there between 2011 and 2015, with thousands more dying from torture and neglect.

On 8 December 2024, the Assad regime collapsed after a 13-day offensive by opposition forces led by Hayat Tahrir al-Sham (HTS) and allied factions. Bashar al-Assad fled to Russia, ending 54 years of Assad family rule. The transitional government led by Prime Minister Mohammad al-Bashir — appointed by HTS leader Ahmad al-Sharaa (formerly Abu Mohammad al-Jolani) — immediately faced the challenge of rebuilding state institutions, including the entire judicial system, from near-zero legitimacy. In March 2025, the Syria Justice and Accountability Centre (SJAC) published its comprehensive "State of Justice in Syria 2025" report documenting the transition: all Assad-era judges were suspended, regime-aligned court structures dissolved, and an investigative committee established to vet judicial personnel. The Syrian Network for Human Rights (SNHR) published in April 2025 a detailed roadmap calling for restructuring the Supreme Judicial Council, establishing a transitional justice tribunal, and creating an independent prosecutorial service — all of which remained partially implemented as of May 2026.

Syria's violence statistics under the Assad regime were among the most extreme in modern history — but measuring them as conventional homicide rates is methodologically inappropriate given the civil war context (2011–2024). The Syrian Observatory for Human Rights (SOHR) documented over 618,000 deaths in the conflict between 2011 and December 2024, of which approximately 350,000 were civilians. In the post-Assad period (December 2024 – May 2026), the SJAC and SNHR documented a sharp reduction in mass political violence, though localised armed clashes, criminal violence, and residual IS (Islamic State) attacks in the eastern provinces persisted. The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) reported in early 2026 that Syria still hosted over 7.2 million internally displaced persons — the world's largest IDP population — while approximately 6 million Syrians remained refugees abroad.

Corruption & Accountability

Syria scores 12 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — the 4th worst score in the world (alongside Equatorial Guinea, Eritrea, Libya, and Yemen at 13, and below North Korea at 15 and Sudan at 15) — ranking 177th out of 180 countries. The CPI 2025 (February 2026) shows a notable improvement to 15/100, rank 172/182 — the largest single-year CPI improvement in Syria's history — reflecting the perception-based impact of Assad's fall and the initial transitional governance reforms. Transparency International notes this represents "the perception of change" following December 2024's political transition rather than verified institutional anti-corruption progress, which remains nascent. Under Assad, corruption was structurally embedded in every layer of the state: the "wasta" (connection) system, military and security service rackets, regime monopolies over import and construction sectors controlled by Assad's cousin Rami Makhlouf (later purged in 2020 in an intra-regime power struggle), and the systematic extortion of humanitarian aid flows, generated one of the most thoroughly corrupt states in recorded history.

The Atef Najib trial — opened in Damascus in April 2025 — became the first domestic accountability proceeding against an Assad-era official: Najib, a cousin of Bashar al-Assad and former head of the Political Security Directorate in Deraa, faces charges of torture, forced disappearance, and crimes against humanity for his role in triggering the Syrian civil war in March 2011 by detaining and torturing children who had painted anti-regime graffiti. The trial, conducted by the new transitional judicial structures, was monitored by the SJAC and UN Special Envoy Geir Pedersen as an early test of Syria's capacity for domestic accountability. International proceedings continue in parallel: Germany's Federal Prosecutor has the world's largest active dossier of Syrian war crimes prosecutions under universal jurisdiction (over 12 ongoing trials as of 2025), France maintains proceedings against Assad himself for crimes against humanity, and the ICC Pre-Trial Chamber is examining jurisdiction over the 2013 chemical weapons attacks in Ghouta.

Institutional Landmark
Palais de Justice — Damas, Syrie

Le Palais de Justice de Damas, bâtiment gouvernemental de style néo-oriental avec ses arches caractéristiques sur la place principale, abritait les juridictions pénales et civiles du régime Assad. Depuis le procès d'Atef Najib — cousin de Bashar al-Assad — ouvert en avril 2025 pour torture et crimes contre l'humanité, il est devenu le symbole de la justice transitionnelle syrienne. C'est le premier grand procès domestique d'un responsable du régime tenu dans ce même bâtiment où des milliers de Syriens ont été condamnés par un appareil judiciaire inféodé au pouvoir politique durant 54 ans de règne Assad.
