The Judicial Framework

Japan's modern legal system is founded on the 1947 Constitution (Nihonkoku Kenpō), drafted under Allied occupation to embed democratic governance, fundamental rights, and the separation of powers following World War II. The system belongs to the civil law tradition, shaped primarily by German and French legal models adopted during the Meiji-era modernisation of the 1880s, though certain areas — particularly commercial and procedural law — reflect later American influence. The judiciary is organised in four tiers: Summary Courts (kan'i saibansho) for minor matters, District Courts (chihō saibansho) as the primary courts of first instance with 50 locations across all 47 prefectures, High Courts (kōtō saibansho) in eight major cities exercising appellate jurisdiction, and the Supreme Court (Saikō-Saibansho) in Tokyo as the apex institution with constitutional review authority.

The Supreme Court of Japan comprises fifteen Justices — including the Chief Justice, appointed by the Emperor on Cabinet recommendation — divided into three Petty Benches of five Justices each for routine appeals, and a Grand Bench of all fifteen for constitutional questions or cases departing from prior precedent. In a uniquely democratic feature of the Japanese constitution, Supreme Court Justices are subject to popular review at the first general election following their appointment and every ten years thereafter, a mechanism of direct public accountability without parallel in most democratic judicial systems. In August 2024, Justice Imasaki was inaugurated as the new Chief Justice, continuing Japan's tradition of quiet, professionalised judicial leadership.

Japan records one of the world's lowest homicide rates. The 2024 White Paper on Crime published by Japan's Ministry of Justice recorded 912 homicides in 2023 — a rate of approximately 0.73 per 100,000 population for a nation of 125 million. While this represents a modest year-on-year increase of 59 cases, Japan remains globally renowned for its public safety, a result of strict gun control legislation, high social cohesion, effective community policing (koban), and culturally embedded respect for law. Japan is consistently cited alongside Singapore and Iceland as among the safest large countries in the world.

Corruption & Accountability

Japan scores 71 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 18th globally — unchanged from 2023, placing it joint with Bhutan and just ahead of the United Kingdom (71, 20th). Japan's score reflects a robust public administration culture shaped by strong bureaucratic traditions, relatively transparent government procurement, and a highly educated, civic-minded citizenry. The country benefits from an independent judiciary, a free press, and effective audit institutions including the Board of Audit of Japan (Kaikei Kensain), one of the world's oldest supreme audit institutions, established in 1880.

Japan faces persistent structural anti-corruption challenges centred on the opaque relationship between large corporations, the ruling Liberal Democratic Party (LDP), and the senior bureaucracy — the so-called "iron triangle." In 2023–2024, a major political fundraising scandal involving LDP faction slush funds (the "uragane" scandal) led to the resignation of multiple Cabinet ministers, the dissolution of all LDP factions, and criminal investigations by the Tokyo District Public Prosecutors Office. This scandal, widely covered internationally, reinforced concerns about political financing transparency and contributed to ongoing public debate about strengthening Japan's Political Funds Control Act, which critics regard as one of the weakest in any major democracy.

Institutional Landmark
最高裁判所 — Saikō-Saibansho, Chiyoda, Tokyo

The Supreme Court of Japan, completed in 1974 and designed by architect Shinichi Okada, occupies a modernist stone complex in the Hayabusachō district of Chiyoda, Tokyo — adjacent to the National Diet Building. Its fifteen Justices exercise ultimate constitutional authority, and uniquely among the world's apex courts, are subject to direct popular referendum at each general election, ensuring democratic accountability at the highest level of the judiciary.
