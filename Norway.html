Norway

Regional Hub Nordic-European Jurisdiction

The Judicial Framework

Norway's legal system is grounded in civil law tradition and a robust constitutional framework established by the Constitution of 1814 — one of the oldest written constitutions in the world still in force. The Norwegian judiciary is fully independent from the executive and legislative branches, with the Supreme Court (Høyesterett) serving as the highest court of appeal. According to the World Justice Project Rule of Law Index 2024, Norway ranks 2nd out of 142 countries globally, excelling in constraints on government powers, fundamental rights, and the absence of corruption.

The Norwegian criminal justice system is internationally recognised as a global model for rehabilitation-centred corrections. The Norwegian Correctional Service (Kriminalomsorgen) operates on the principle that the purpose of incarceration is to reduce reoffending, not simply to punish. Facilities like Halden Prison — often cited as the world's most humane prison — offer inmates individual cells, educational programmes, vocational training, and psychological support. Norway's recidivism rate stands at approximately 20% within two years of release, one of the lowest in the world, compared to over 60% in many other developed nations (UNODC, 2023).

Norway's intentional homicide rate is among the lowest globally, recorded at 0.53 per 100,000 population (UNODC, 2023) — a figure consistent with the country's longstanding low levels of violent crime, strong social welfare system, and high levels of institutional trust. The Norwegian Police Service (Politiet) operates under a community policing model that prioritises de-escalation and crime prevention over enforcement.

Corruption & Accountability

Norway scores 81 out of 100 on Transparency International's 2024 Corruption Perceptions Index, ranking 4th globally — alongside New Zealand, and just below Finland (#2) and Denmark (#1). The country benefits from an exceptionally transparent public administration, a strong freedom of information culture, and stringent anti-corruption legislation under the Norwegian Penal Code (Sections 387–389).

Norway's commitment to open government is reinforced by the Norwegian Parliament (Storting) Act on Public Access to Documents, which grants citizens broad rights to inspect government records. ØKOKRIM — the Norwegian National Authority for Investigation and Prosecution of Economic and Environmental Crime — plays a central role in investigating corruption, money laundering, and financial crime at both national and international levels, and has been instrumental in prosecuting high-profile corporate corruption cases.
