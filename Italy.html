Judiciary Under Reform

Italy

Repubblica Italiana Civil Law — Southern Europe

The Judicial Framework

Italy operates a civil law system rooted in the Napoleonic tradition, with the Codice Civile of 1942 and the Codice di Procedura Penale of 1989 forming the twin pillars of its private and criminal law. The Italian judicial system is divided into ordinary courts — headed by the Corte Suprema di Cassazione (Supreme Court of Cassation), housed in the monumental Palazzo di Giustizia in Rome — and specialised jurisdictions including administrative courts (headed by the Consiglio di Stato), audit courts (Corte dei Conti), and regional administrative tribunals (TAR). The Consiglio Superiore della Magistratura (CSM), a constitutional body chaired by the President of the Republic, governs the careers, appointments, and discipline of the approximately 8,500 professional judges and public prosecutors who together form the "magistracy" — a unified body whose independence from the executive is constitutionally guaranteed under Article 104.

Italy's most significant constitutional development in decades occurred on 30 October 2025, when the Italian Parliament approved a landmark constitutional reform of the magistracy — the so-called "separazione delle carriere" reform — by a razor-thin majority. The reform, championed by Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni's coalition, constitutionally separates judges and prosecutors into two distinct professional orders, creates a separate CSM for each order, and establishes a new disciplinary court (the "Alto Consiglio della Magistratura") supervised by politicians. Critics — including the Italian Association of Magistrates, the Constitutional Court, and the Verfassungsblog — warn the reform structurally weakens prosecutorial independence and was designed to limit the magistracy's ability to investigate the political class. Because it is a constitutional amendment, the reform is now subject to a confirmatory referendum, expected in 2026, making it the most consequential Italian judicial referendum since the 1987 vote on magistrates' civil liability.

Italy recorded 327 intentional homicides in 2024 — a rate of 0.55 per 100,000 population, down 2.1% from 334 in 2023 — the lowest homicide rate in Italy's modern statistical history, according to ISTAT's February 2026 bulletin. Of the 327 victims, 116 were women and 211 were men; 62 women were killed by a current or former partner, making intimate-partner femicide the dominant form of female homicide (53.4% of female victims). ISTAT confirms Italy has the lowest homicide rate in the EU alongside Slovenia, with a rate of 0.55 per 100,000 against an EU average of 0.91. This extraordinary safety record coexists with the continued presence of organised crime networks — the Camorra (Campania), 'Ndrangheta (Calabria), and Cosa Nostra (Sicily) — whose violence is increasingly contained within inter-clan rivalries rather than affecting the general population, following decades of sustained anti-mafia judicial operations.

Corruption & Accountability

Italy scores 53 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024, ranking 52nd globally — a decline of 1 point from 54 in 2023, continuing a decade of stagnation below the 60-point threshold. Transparency International's Western Europe & EU 2024 analysis attributes Italy's persistent underperformance to a combination of slow justice, opaque lobbying practices, endemic conflicts of interest in public procurement, and the weakening of anti-corruption legislative safeguards under the Meloni government — which in 2023 reduced mandatory prison terms for corruption offences and limited the scope of the "abuse of office" crime (abuso d'ufficio), since partially reinstated. Italy now ranks 23rd out of 27 EU member states in the CPI.

Italy's anti-corruption architecture includes the Autorità Nazionale Anticorruzione (ANAC), established in 2014 under the so-called Legge Severino framework, which monitors public procurement, whistleblower protection, and conflict-of-interest declarations. The Procura della Repubblica — especially the specialised anti-mafia and anti-corruption pool prosecutors (DDA and DDAG) — have conducted landmark investigations including Tangentopoli (1992–1994), Mani Pulite, and the ongoing 'Ndrangheta trials. Italy's Corte Costituzionale was particularly active in 2025, ruling on citizenship by descent (Judgment No. 142/2025 — upholding the constitutional validity of automatic jure sanguinis conferral until its 2025 legislative reform), and is set to review the magistracy reform's constitutionality in 2026 if confirmed by referendum.

Institutional Landmark
Palazzo di Giustizia — Corte Suprema di Cassazione, Rome

Le Palazzo di Giustizia de Rome — surnommé le "Palazzaccio" par les Romains — est l'un des monuments néoclassiques les plus imposants d'Europe. Construit entre 1888 et 1910 sur les rives du Tibre, il abrite la Corte Suprema di Cassazione, la Biblioteca Giuridica della Corte, et la Procura Generale. Ses façades ornées de statues allégoriques de la Justice et de bustes de grands juristes italiens, et son quadrige triomphal culminant le fronton principal, en font l'expression architecturale la plus grandiose de l'État de droit dans la tradition juridique romaine.
