Yemen

الجمهورية اليمنية Mixed Law — Middle East

The Judicial Framework

Yemen's legal system is a hybrid of civil law, Islamic sharia, and customary tribal law, governed by the Constitution of 1994 and the Judicial Authority Law of 1991. Prior to the civil war (2014–present), the formal judicial hierarchy ran from First Instance Courts through Courts of Appeal to the Supreme Court in Sanaa — the apex body for cassation, constitutional review, and final appellate jurisdiction. Since the Houthi takeover of Sanaa in September 2014 and the subsequent Saudi-led coalition intervention from March 2015, Yemen has effectively operated two parallel judicial systems: the Houthi-controlled judiciary in Sanaa governing northern and western Yemen (approximately 70% of the population), and the Presidential Leadership Council (PLC)-aligned judiciary based in Aden and southern/eastern governorates. The internationally recognised government (IRG/PLC) maintains nominal judicial structures in Aden, but the US State Department's 2025 Trafficking in Persons report notes these institutions "largely did not function due to infrastructure damage" — with courts in many PLC-controlled areas operating sporadically or not at all.

The Houthi movement (Ansar Allah) has systematically dismantled what remained of Yemen's judicial independence in areas under its control. In September 2024, Houthi authorities amended 14 articles of the Judicial Authority Law, redistributing powers to grant the Supreme Political Council direct authority to appoint judges and members of the Supreme Judicial Council — described by the Yemeni Bar Association and Judges Club in Sanaa as a "judicial massacre" and a return to the pre-republican Imamate era. The amendments were followed in November 2025 by Circular No. 48, placing 83 graduates of Houthi religious indoctrination centres as trainee judges in commercial and administrative courts across 8 governorates — institutionalising the "ideological judge" model explicitly modelled on Iran's Wilayat al-Faqih principle. The Yemen Centre for Yemeni Studies (WCYS) documented this transformation in November 2025 as "courts turned from a refuge for justice into tools for legitimising arrests and property seizures under a legal façade." In PLC-controlled southern Yemen, the Southern Transitional Council (STC) maintains its own parallel legal structures in Aden, further fragmenting the judicial landscape.

Yemen's conflict has produced one of the worst humanitarian catastrophes in modern history. ACLED's expanded Yemen dataset (updated July and December 2025) added over 4,337 new events and at least 3,474 previously unrecorded fatalities to its historical dataset — confirming the scale of underreporting. In Q1 2025 (January–March), ECOI/ACLED recorded 1,079 security incidents in Yemen including 482 battles, 284 explosions/remote violence events, and 119 civilian targeting incidents. Q3 2024 (July–September) and Q4 2024 (October–December) saw a significant escalation following Israeli strikes on Houthi infrastructure in retaliation for Houthi drone and missile attacks on Israel — attacks that drew Yemen deeper into the regional conflict dimension of the Gaza war. The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA) estimated in early 2026 that over 21.6 million Yemenis — approximately 65% of the population — require humanitarian assistance, making Yemen the world's largest humanitarian crisis by need severity.

Corruption & Accountability

Yemen scores 13 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — ranking 173rd out of 180 countries, alongside Equatorial Guinea and Libya at the same score. This represents a three-point collapse from 16/100 (rank 176) in 2023, and the worst score Yemen has ever recorded since TI began assessing it. The CPI 2025 (February 2026) maintained the score at 13/100 but the rank fell to 177th out of 182 as the expanded index placed Yemen solidly among the five most corrupt nations globally — alongside Libya, Venezuela, Somalia, and South Sudan. Yemen's dual-authority structure has compounded corruption on both sides: the Houthi government has captured all state revenue streams in the north (customs, port fees, telecommunications), while the PLC/IRG has been documented by the UN Panel of Experts as systematically diverting international aid and Saudi Coalition transfers for patronage and personal enrichment.

Yemen's accountability mechanisms have been reduced to international instruments: the UN Panel of Experts on Yemen (established under UNSC Resolution 2140, 2014) — whose mandate was controversially not renewed in February 2024 after Russia and China vetoed its extension — served as the primary accountability body for over a decade, documenting arms embargo violations, war crimes, and corruption by all parties. Its lapse leaves a critical accountability vacuum. The UN Group of Eminent International and Regional Experts on Yemen (GEE), established by the Human Rights Council, documented potential war crimes by the Houthi forces, Saudi-led coalition, and STC through 2023 — but its mandate was also terminated. Active accountability proceedings include ICC preliminary examinations and universal jurisdiction cases in Germany and Belgium targeting Houthi officials for torture and enforced disappearance. A 2026 Case Western Reserve University study documented the systematic use of torture by all Yemeni factions as a potential crime against humanity — constituting the most comprehensive legal analysis of individual criminal responsibility in the Yemen conflict to date.

Institutional Context
Vieille ville de Sanaa — Capitale disputée, Yémen

Sanaa, l'une des plus anciennes cités habitées du monde et classée au patrimoine mondial de l'UNESCO, est la capitale constitutionnelle du Yémen et le siège de la Cour suprême — aujourd'hui sous contrôle Houthi. L'architecture millénaire de ses tours en pisé aux fenêtres décorées de plâtre blanc contraste brutalement avec la réalité d'un système judiciaire en cours de démantèlement idéologique : en 2024–2025, les Houthis ont amendé 14 articles de la loi sur le pouvoir judiciaire et placé 83 diplômés de leurs centres religieux comme juges stagiaires, transformant les tribunaux en instruments du mouvement Ansar Allah.
