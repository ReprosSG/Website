Somalia

The Judicial Framework

Somalia operates a mixed legal system combining civil law derived from Italian and British colonial traditions, Islamic sharia law (applied in personal status, family, and inheritance matters), and customary clan law (xeer) — the last of which governs the vast majority of disputes outside Mogadishu. The Federal Government of Somalia, re-established in 2012 after two decades of state collapse (1991–2012), has been rebuilding its formal judicial institutions under the Provisional Federal Constitution (2012). The judicial hierarchy runs from District Courts and Regional Courts through Courts of Appeal to the apex Federal Supreme Court, located in Mogadishu. The Judicial Service Commission (JSC) — established under Article 111 of the Provisional Federal Constitution — is responsible for judicial appointments, discipline, and oversight, though a Heritage Institute study (January 2025) identified the JSC model as structurally ill-suited to Somalia's federal reality: the JSC was designed for a unified legal framework but operates in a country where federal member states (Jubaland, South West, Hirshabelle, Galmudug, Puntland) maintain parallel, largely autonomous judicial systems. Prime Minister Hamza Abdi Barre laid the foundation stone for a new Supreme Court building in Mogadishu in 2024 — the first purpose-built apex court facility in the country's history.

Somalia's judiciary is in an active phase of reconstruction under the Federal Government's National Development Plan 8 (2020–2024) and its successor framework. In 2025, the Mogadishu judiciary welcomed a cohort of newly sworn judges — the largest single intake since the re-establishment of federal institutions — in a ceremony marking the expansion of active bench capacity. The Supreme Court published a brief overview of the Somali judicial system in August 2025 identifying three structural pillars of reform: unification of the multi-system legal order (civil law, sharia, xeer), federalisation of judicial governance to align with the 2012 constitution's federal structure, and capacity building for a judiciary that faces acute shortages of qualified lawyers. In al-Shabaab-controlled territories (estimated at 15–20% of national territory as of 2025), the group maintains its own sharia-based court system enforcing strict interpretations incompatible with international human rights standards — a parallel justice system that has operated continuously since 2006.

Somalia is the most violent country in the ACLED dataset when measured by number of violent events — the ninth most fatal globally. In the period April 2023 – March 2025, ACLED recorded 5,944 security incidents resulting in 14,883 estimated fatalities (combatants and civilians), including 3,759 battles, 1,479 explosions/remote violence, and 706 direct attacks on civilians. Al-Shabaab remains the primary perpetrator — responsible for an estimated 65–70% of all security incidents — with clan violence, IS-Somalia, and intercommunal conflict accounting for the remainder. The EUAA (EU Asylum Agency) 2025 country of origin report notes that clan violence reached its highest recorded level in 2024, based on migration authority data. INSO's Conflict and Humanitarian Data Centre documented 6,170 directly impacted civilians (killed, injured, abducted, or arrested) in the same period — a figure considered an undercount given limited access for monitors in conflict zones.

Corruption & Accountability

Somalia scores 9 out of 100 on the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index 2024 — a decline of 2 points from 11 in 2023 — ranking 179th out of 180 countries, the second worst globally after South Sudan (8). The CPI 2025 (February 2026) maintained the score at 9/100 but the expanded index placed Somalia at rank 181/182 — only South Sudan (8/100) ranks lower. Somalia has been among the three lowest CPI scorers in the world every single year since TI first assessed it in 2012. Corruption in Somalia is structural and multi-layered: at the federal level, diversion of customs revenues and port fees (the Port of Mogadishu is Somalia's primary revenue source), ghost worker payrolls in security forces, and aid diversion have been extensively documented by the UN Monitoring Group on Somalia. At the local level, al-Shabaab's extensive extortion network — collecting zakaat (Islamic taxation) from businesses, road checkpoints, and agricultural land — generates an estimated USD 80–100 million annually, making it one of the world's best-funded non-state armed groups.

Somalia's accountability framework centres on the Office of the Attorney General, the Financial Governance Committee (FGC — a joint oversight body with international donors), and the Benadir Regional Court which handles the most significant criminal cases in Mogadishu. The UN Security Council's Somalia Sanctions Committee maintains asset freezes and travel bans on designated al-Shabaab and IS-Somalia leaders. The most significant recent accountability development is the Federal Government's anti-corruption campaign launched by President Hassan Sheikh Mohamud in 2023–2024, which resulted in the dismissal of several senior officials for financial misconduct — though Transparency International Somalia (transparency.so) notes these actions remain politically selective and insufficiently institutional. Somalia's 2024 CPI decline from 11 to 9 points is attributed by TI to the deterioration of anti-corruption enforcement quality and the persistence of clan-based patronage networks that undermine meritocratic public administration.

Institutional Landmark
Judiciary of Somalia — Mogadishu

La cérémonie d'assermentation de nouveaux juges à Mogadiscio — la plus importante depuis la reconstruction des institutions fédérales — symbolise la renaissance judiciaire en cours en Somalie depuis la réfédéralisation de 2012. Les robes bleu clair des magistrats contrastent avec la réalité d'un système judiciaire qui opère sans bâtiment propre jusqu'à la pose de la première pierre de la nouvelle Cour suprême en 2024. Face à un pays où al-Shabaab maintient des tribunaux parallèles sur 15 à 20% du territoire national, la reconstruction de la justice fédérale représente un enjeu existentiel pour l'État somalien.
